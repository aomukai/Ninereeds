## Justification

### What justification is

Justification is giving a good reason for a claim, choice, or action. When a child says "I think the seed will grow because it has water and sun," that child is giving justification. Justification helps other people understand why an answer makes sense. It turns a bare answer into an explained answer.

### Sources of justification

A person can justify a claim in different ways:

**From seeing:** "I know the cup fell because I saw it fall." Direct observation is one of the strongest sources of justification. The speaker was there and watched what happened.

**From being told:** "I know bears sleep in winter because the teacher said so." Learning from a trusted person is another source. The speaker did not see it, but a reliable source shared the information.

**From reasoning:** "I know five plus three is eight because I counted." The speaker figured it out by thinking it through. Reasoning uses what we already know to reach a new answer.

**From checking:** "I know the answer is right because I checked it twice." Going back to look again adds strength to justification.

### When justification is weak

Not all justification is strong. Some justification does not help much:

- "Because I want it to be true" — Wanting is not justification.
- "Because I said so" — This tells us nothing about why.
- "Because everyone says so" — Many people being wrong together is still wrong.
- "Because it seems that way" — A feeling is not the same as a reason.

Good justification points to something outside of just the speaker's wish or feeling. It points to the world, to rules, or to things that can be checked.

### Justification in school

Teachers often ask children to justify answers. A teacher may ask, "How do you know?" or "What makes you think that?" These questions invite the child to give justification.

A child who says "I think the plant needs light" gives a guess. A child who says "I think the plant needs light because the plant near the window grew bigger" gives justification. The second answer connects the claim to something that was seen.

### Justification is not a guess

A guess is an answer without support. Justification is an answer with support. A child can change a guess into a justified claim by adding a reason, an observation, or an example.

## Reason Why

### What a reason why is

A reason why is the explanation for something. It answers the question "why?" A reason why connects an answer to what happened in the world, what someone knows, or what makes sense.

### Different kinds of reasons

**Causal reasons:** These explain what made something happen. "The ball rolled because I pushed it." The push is the reason why the ball moved.

**Logical reasons:** These explain why something must be true. "The shape has four sides, so it must be a square or a rectangle." The four sides give a reason for the conclusion.

**Personal reasons:** These explain why someone made a choice. "I picked the red cup because red is my favorite color." The preference is the reason for the choice.

### Reasons come from different places

A reason can come from:
- What someone saw or heard
- What someone remembers
- What someone was told by a trusted person
- What someone figured out by thinking

Each of these can be a real reason why. The listener decides if the reason is strong enough.

### Reason vs. example

A reason why explains how or why something is true. An example shows one case where something is true. These are different jobs.

- Reason: "Plants need water because water helps them stay alive."
- Example: "The flower by the sink grew because it got water every day."

The reason tells why in general. The example shows one case where it happened.

### Giving reasons in conversation

When someone asks "why?", they are asking for a reason. A good answer gives a reason that connects the claim to something the listener can understand or check. A child who practices giving reasons becomes better at explaining thinking.

## Example

### What an example is

An example is one case that helps show an idea clearly. If someone says "some animals have fur," a dog or a cat is an example. The example is not the whole idea, but it makes the idea easier to see.

### Why examples help

Examples take a big or general idea and show one clear case. This helps the listener picture what the idea means. A child learning about "cold places" may understand better when told "like the North Pole or a refrigerator." The examples make the idea concrete.

### Good examples vs. weak examples

A good example fits the idea clearly and is easy to understand. A weak example is confusing, unusual, or does not fit well.

**Good example:** "Fruits include apples and oranges." Both are common, clearly fruits, and easy to picture.

**Weak example:** "Fruits include tomatoes." This is technically true, but confusing because many people think of tomatoes as vegetables.

**Unusual example:** "Fruits include durian." This is true, but if the listener does not know what durian is, it does not help.

Good examples are common, clear, and fit the idea without extra explaining.

### How many examples are enough

One example is better than none. Two or three examples can show that an idea applies in more than one place. Too many examples can make talking take too long.

A child giving examples in class usually needs one or two clear ones. More is not always better.

### Examples are not proof

An example shows that something can happen. It does not show that it always happens or must happen. If a child says "my dog likes baths, so all dogs like baths," that is too big a jump. One example does not prove a rule about all cases.

## That Proves

### What "that proves" means

"That proves" means something shows an idea is true in a strong way. The speaker is saying the support is so clear that the claim must be right. It points to a result, a sign, or a fact that makes the claim hard to doubt.

### When "that proves" is strong

"That proves" is strong when the support clearly leads to the claim:

- "The ground is wet and there are puddles everywhere. That proves it rained." The wet ground and puddles are signs that point strongly to rain.
- "I found the missing crayon in my pocket. That proves I had it all along." Finding the object is direct evidence.

In these cases, the support fits the claim tightly.

### When "that proves" is too strong

Sometimes people say "that proves" when the support is not actually that strong:

- "She smiled at me. That proves she wants to be my friend." A smile can mean many things, not just friendship.
- "I got the answer right once. That proves I am the smartest." One right answer does not prove being smartest.

When "that proves" is used for weak support, the claim sounds bigger than the evidence allows.

### Prove vs. support

**Prove:** The support is so strong that the claim must be true. There is no other good explanation.
**Support:** The support makes the claim more likely to be true, but does not rule out other explanations.

Many things we call "proof" are really strong support. True proving is harder than it seems. A careful speaker knows the difference.

### Using "that proves" in school

In class, children sometimes say "that proves" when they mean "that shows" or "that supports." Teachers may help by asking: "Does it prove it, or just make it more likely?" This helps children learn to use evidence words carefully.

## How these concepts connect

- **Justification** is the big skill: giving good reasons for what you say.
- A **reason why** is one kind of support used in justification.
- An **example** is another kind of support that shows one case clearly.
- **That proves** is a phrase for when the support is very strong.

All four work together. A child giving justification may give a reason why, add an example, and say "that proves my point." Strong thinkers learn to use all these tools.

## Common child-facing scenarios

### In the classroom

**Teacher:** "Why do you think the ice melted?"
**Child:** "I think it melted because it was near the heater." (giving a reason why)
**Child:** "For example, the ice cube on the table is still there, but the one by the heater is gone." (giving an example)
**Child:** "That proves the heater made it melt." (claiming strong support)

### During an argument

**Child A:** "That's not fair!"
**Child B:** "Yes it is, and I can give justification. You went first last time, so it's my turn now."

The justification connects the claim ("it is fair") to a reason (taking turns means the other person goes now).

### When explaining a mistake

**Child:** "I thought the box was empty, but I was wrong."
**Adult:** "What made you think it was empty?"
**Child:** "Because I shook it and heard nothing. But the reason why I was wrong is that the thing inside was soft and did not make a sound."

The child gives justification for the original thought and a reason why the justification turned out to be misleading.

## Summary

Evidence and justification concepts help children move from guessing to explaining. They learn to give reasons, choose good examples, and understand when support is strong or weak. These skills grow slowly with practice.
