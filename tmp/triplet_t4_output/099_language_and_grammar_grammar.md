The teacher asks the class to read the sentence aloud, but Noah cannot tell where one part ends. "If a sentence has no punctuation, then it is hard to understand," Noah says to his friend.

The teacher nods and walks to the board. She picks up a piece of chalk and adds a comma after the first part of the sentence. Then she adds a period right at the end. Noah watches and sees how the punctuation marks make the words clear. Now the sentence has a pause and a stop, and every part makes sense.

After the teacher finishes, Noah takes out his own paper. He writes a short sentence and carefully adds a comma and a period in the right places. His friend looks at the paper and asks, "How did you know where to put the marks?" Noah points to the board and explains that punctuation shows where to pause and where to stop.

Finally, the teacher reads Noah's work and smiles because every mark is in the right place. Noah feels proud that his writing is clear and correct. He understands that good grammar needs proper punctuation to work well. If he uses punctuation the right way, then his sentences will always make sense.
