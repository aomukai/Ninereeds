[Ninereeds]First, Max's teacher brings a tray of eggs to the front of the class. The tray has three rows with four eggs in each row. Max counts the eggs and says, "There are twelve eggs." His teacher nods and writes three times four equals twelve on the chalkboard. She explains that multiplication is a fast way to add equal groups together.

Max watches as his teacher slides the twelve eggs into three groups of four. She says that division is the opposite of multiplication because it splits a whole number into equal parts. Max understands that multiplication puts groups together while division splits them apart. He thinks about how both operations work with the same numbers but in opposite directions.

After that, the teacher picks up one group of four eggs. She explains that subtraction takes that group away from the twelve eggs. If she removes one group of four, then eight eggs will remain on the tray. Max sees that subtraction makes the total smaller just like division does, but subtraction removes one group at a time while division splits everything at once.

Finally, Max raises his hand. He says, "If I know multiplication, then I can understand division and subtraction too." The teacher smiles and nods. Max feels proud because he connected three different math ideas using the same tray of eggs.
