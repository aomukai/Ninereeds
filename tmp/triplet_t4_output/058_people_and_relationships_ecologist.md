[Ninereeds]First, Luke the ecologist knelt beside the stream. Water flowed past the oak roots and between the moss-covered stones. He pulled a notebook from his pack and wrote down what he saw. "If the roots hold the bank in place," he said, "then the water will stay clear." The flowing water, the stones, and the tree roots all belonged to one ecosystem.

He watched a frog sit on a rock near the bank and a bird land on a branch overhead. Every creature and every plant depended on the same ecosystem. Luke understood that each part played a role in keeping the water clean and the soil rich.

After that, Luke walked downstream to a bend in the stream. He noted where the bank had washed away and recorded his observations. If the roots did not hold the soil, then the sediment would cloud the water. Conservation meant choosing the right action to protect the whole forest.

Finally, Luke closed his notebook and smiled. The woods were peaceful and the stream was clear. Conservation was the work of understanding every connection in the ecosystem. He stood and walked back along the trail.
