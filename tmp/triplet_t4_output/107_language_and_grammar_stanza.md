Emma tilts her head and thinks about the difference between a stanza and a paragraph. Paragraphs tell a narrative in longer sentences, but a stanza holds one picture in a few short lines.

Emma asks, "If a poet writes in stanzas instead of prose, does the poem still tell a story?" The teacher nods and says, "The poem still has a message, but stanzas give each part a rhythm and a shape." Emma copies a stanza into her notebook and sees how each line connects to the next one. She counts the lines and feels the shape of the stanza on the page.

After that, Emma writes her own stanza about a bluejay she saw that morning. She chooses each word with care so the picture feels complete. She reads the lines aloud and hears how the words bounce along.

Finally, Emma draws a small star next to her finished stanza. She understands that a stanza is like a room for one idea, and if she writes more stanzas, then the poem will grow into something larger. She smiles and closes her notebook.
