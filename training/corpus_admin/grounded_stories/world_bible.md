# Ninereeds Story World — Generation Guide

This document is the canonical reference for all grounded story generation.
Every story DeepSeek writes for this corpus must be consistent with the cast,
locations, and writing rules below.

---

## The Cast

### Emma
A girl, around eight years old. Curious and careful — she notices small things
(a crack in the bark, a trail of ants, the way dew sits on a leaf). She likes
picking things up and turning them over in her hands. She asks questions when
something puzzles her.

### Taro
A boy, same age as Emma. More physical and impulsive — he climbs before he
thinks, jumps into puddles without checking how deep, runs ahead on the path.
But he listens when Gran explains something. His full name in Japanese is 太郎;
in English and German stories he is Taro.

### Gran
Emma and Taro's grandmother. She lives in the stone cottage. She is the one
who knows what things are and what they become — she tells them that the acorn
grows into an oak, that the cocoon will open, that the clouds coming from the
west mean rain by afternoon. She does not lecture. She answers questions while
doing something else: kneading dough, pulling weeds, hanging laundry.

### Biscuit
A medium-sized brown dog. He belongs to the cottage and follows whoever is
going somewhere interesting. He sniffs everything, barks at squirrels, splashes
through puddles without hesitation, and carries sticks back that are too big
for him. He cannot speak but his actions often move the story forward — he
finds the squirrel hole, he pulls toward the pond, he comes back soaked and
shakes water everywhere.

### Bello
A smaller dog, younger than Biscuit, who arrives in spring (story 48). Black
and white, with ears that stand up like little flags. Where Biscuit is
confident and nosy, Bello is quick and watchful — he darts into gaps, chases
things Biscuit has already lost interest in, and circles back to check on the
children. He and Biscuit took a full afternoon to settle with each other; now
they run together. His name in German stories is Bello; in Japanese ベロ;
in Chinese 贝洛.

## Anchor Locations

All stories are set in or near these places. They are always described
consistently — same features, same physical details, same sensory qualities.

### 1. The Oak
A very old oak tree at the edge of Gran's field, where the grass meets the
trees. It has one low branch thick enough to sit on, about shoulder height on
a child. The roots are large and raised; a squirrel has a hole between two of
them at the base. The bark is rough and deeply furrowed. In autumn the ground
around it is covered in acorns and dead leaves. In winter it is bare. In spring
it has small bright-green leaves. A robin sometimes nests in the fork higher up.

**Appears in:** oak tree, rainy day (shelter under it), bird's nest (the upper
fork), autumn leaves, oak in snow.

### 2. Gran's House
A low stone cottage at the end of a lane. The kitchen has a wooden table, a
heavy iron pot that hangs over the fire, a window over the sink looking out
onto the garden, and a smell of bread or soup almost always. The garden behind
the cottage has a vegetable patch, an apple tree, a stone bench, and a low
wooden gate. The garden shed leans against the back wall and is full of tools.

**Appears in:** kitchen cooking, baking, morning routine, getting dressed,
bedtime, rainy window, winter evening, garden, apple orchard, birthday,
building/making (shed), morning garden.

### 3. The Pond
A shallow pond at the bottom of the field, a short walk from the oak. It has
reeds along one edge, a flat stone good for sitting on, and a willow that
dips into the water on the far side. Frogs sit on the lily pads in summer.
A pair of ducks nests in the reeds in spring. The water is still enough to
reflect the sky when there is no wind. In clear, still weather, small fish
are visible near the bottom from the flat stone — slow-moving, dark, gone
the moment a shadow crosses the water.

A low wooden bench sits a few paces back from the flat stone, under the
willow's edge. Gran uses it. The children use the flat stone. The bench is
old enough that the wood is grey and the grain has risen; it has a slight
lean to one side. On summer evenings the light on the pond turns gold and
the fish come closer to the surface.

**Appears in:** pond life, fishing, picnic (blanket on the grass beside it),
fish watching, dogs at the pond, Gran sitting while children play, evening
at the pond.

### 4. The Meadow Path
A narrow path through long grass that connects Gran's garden gate to the pond
and, further along, to the school. In summer the grass on either side is tall
enough to hide Biscuit. Wildflowers grow in the verges — dandelions, daisies,
clover. The path is muddy after rain. Butterflies cross it in warm weather.

**Appears in:** rainy day (the path floods and turns to mud), meadow/butterfly,
dog walk, body and touch (children run and fall here), farm animals (the field
beside it has sheep).

### 5. The Schoolyard and Classroom
A walled yard at the far end of the meadow path. A green wooden gate, always
slightly stiff. A climbing frame in one corner, a bench along the wall, a big
ash tree that gives shade in summer. The school bell can be heard from the
meadow path on still mornings.

The classroom inside is one large room with tall windows on the south side.
Wooden desks in rows — the tops are carved with old initials. A blackboard
at the front, the chalk ledge dusty and white. The teacher writes in large,
careful letters. The room smells of chalk and old wood and, on cold days, the
iron radiator that clicks and hisses. A nature table stands beside the
window: a jar of tadpoles one week, a pressed leaf collection the next,
whatever the children have brought in. The coat hooks are in the corridor,
labelled with each child's name on a small card.

**Appears in:** schoolyard, playground, arithmetic lesson, nature table,
classroom scenes.

### 6. Gran's Garden (full detail)
The garden behind the cottage is enclosed by a low stone wall on three sides
and the cottage wall on the fourth. It is small enough to see from one end
to the other, large enough to feel separate from the house.

The vegetable patch runs along the south wall. Beans climb up canes tied in
wigwams. Carrots and turnips grow below ground — the children cannot tell
what is there until Gran puts the fork in and lifts. A row of cabbages, tight
and heavy in autumn. Herbs grow in a stone trough by the kitchen door:
rosemary, thyme, something grey-green and sharp that Gran pinches between
her fingers when she is thinking.

The apple tree stands near the centre. In spring it is all blossom and
bees. In autumn the branches bend with fruit. Windfalls land in the grass
and smell sweet and slightly fermented.

The pear tree is trained flat against the east wall, its branches spread
like hands. The pears are small and hard most of the summer; there is
a single week in early autumn when they are right, and then they are gone.

The henhouse is at the far end, behind the apple tree. Three hens. Feathers
on the ground near the door. The smell of warm straw. The eggs are brown
and sometimes still warm when Emma collects them in the morning.

The compost corner is behind the shed — a wooden frame with a lid that
does not quite fit. Gran lifts it with a stick. It steams on cold mornings.
Earthworms thread through the dark material inside.

The stone bench is against the cottage wall near the kitchen door, in a
patch of afternoon sun. Gran sits there to top and tail beans, to drink
tea, sometimes just to watch what is happening in the garden.

**Appears in:** garden morning, planting seeds, apple picking, egg
collecting, compost, herb gathering, pear tree, Gran on the bench.

### 7. The Upstairs
The stairs are steep, with a rope banister on one side worn smooth with
use. The third step from the bottom creaks. Gran's room is at the top on
the right; the door is always slightly open. The children's room is at the
other end of the short landing.

The children's room has a low ceiling — close enough to touch if you
stretch from the bed. Two iron-frame beds with patchwork covers. A chest of
drawers with a small mirror on top; the drawers smell of cedar. A skylight
that can be propped open with a stick on warm nights. Rain on the tiles
sounds very loud and very close. In winter the room is cold until the bodies
in the beds warm it; in summer the morning light comes in sharp and early.
At night the room is dark except for the thin line of light from the lane
under the curtain edge.

**Appears in:** morning routine, bedtime, can't sleep, rainy night, waking
to snow, sick child resting upstairs.

### 8. The Village Lane
A stone-paved lane runs from Gran's garden gate down the hill to the village.
It is narrow — two people can just pass. The hedges on either side are thick
in summer, thin and leafless in winter, and between November and March the
lane is cold and shadowed most of the day. Puddles collect in a dip halfway
down where the stones have sunk; after heavy rain this becomes a small stream
that runs off into the ditch.

From the top of the lane, looking back, you can see Gran's chimney and the
line of the oak at the field's edge. At the bottom the lane opens onto the
village street without warning — a step, a corner, and then the baker's smell
hits.

**Appears in:** walk to the village, running an errand, trip to the vet,
trip to the doctor, coming home in the rain.

### 9. The Village
A single main street with houses on both sides. A baker's with loaves in
the window. A post box set into the wall near the corner — dark red,
slightly mossy at the base. A butcher with a striped awning, a newsagent,
and a small general shop. Further down, a side street runs behind the main
row: narrower, quieter, with the veterinary surgery and the doctor's surgery
facing each other across the lane.

There is a green in the centre with a bench and an old horse chestnut. On
Saturdays a market stall sets up at one end of the street — the one Taro
goes to in the apple story.

The village is close enough to reach on foot; far enough that going there
is a small event, not a daily one.

**Appears in:** market, errand, birthday present, posting a letter, passing
through on the way to somewhere.

### 10. The Veterinary Surgery
A low building at the end of the side street, with a sign above the door
and a low step worn smooth. The waiting room has three plastic chairs, a
shelf of leaflets about pet food and worm treatment, and a noticeboard with
pictures of lost cats. The smell is disinfectant and dog, and underneath it
something sharper. There is usually someone else waiting: an old man with a
terrier, a woman with a cat carrier, once a child holding a shoebox very
carefully.

The vet is a woman with short dark hair and quick, certain hands. She speaks
to the animals before she speaks to the people. She has a floor scale that
she coaxes dogs onto, a metal examination table that is always cold, and
a drawer of instruments that rattle when she opens it. She gives bad news
calmly and good news the same way.

Biscuit submits to the examination with the resignation of long experience.
He does not like the cold table. He weighs more than the vet expects.
Bello, on his first visit, trembles at the door and has to be carried in;
by the time it is over he has eaten two treats from the vet's coat pocket
and is no longer trembling.

**Appears in:** Biscuit's vaccination, Biscuit's claw trim, Bello's first
check-up, weighing the dogs, any routine annual visit.

### 11. The Doctor's Surgery
Directly across the side street from the vet, behind a wooden gate with a
black latch. The waiting room is quieter than the street. Six chairs along
the wall, a low table with old magazines, a window with a net curtain that
lets in light but not sight. A fish tank on the shelf beside the reception
hatch — three orange fish that circle slowly.

The doctor is a tall man who says "Hmm" before he says most other things.
He wears a stethoscope around his neck at all times and has very cold
hands, which he warns about before he uses them. The examination room has
a paper-covered table, a height chart on the wall, a sink, and a light on
a hinge that he shines into ears and throats.

When a child is sick and at Gran's, the usual sequence is: a day on the
sofa under a blanket, Gran's soup brought in a bowl, the curtains half
drawn. If it goes on, Gran takes them to the doctor. They walk down the
lane together. The doctor says "Hmm," examines, writes something on a
small pad. Usually he sends them home. Once or twice he sends them to
the chemist in the main street first.

**Appears in:** Emma's sore throat, Taro's stomach ache, routine
check before school, Gran collecting a prescription, a jab (vaccination)
for the children.

### 12. Sick at Gran's
Not a location but a recurring circumstance with its own sensory register.
It deserves its own entry because several stories turn on it.

The child is on the sitting-room sofa under Gran's heaviest blanket — the
brown one with a satin edge. The curtains are half drawn. The room has a
different quality than usual: the sounds from the kitchen are muffled, the
light is yellow and still, the clock is louder. The child is either very
hot or has just stopped being very hot. Gran brings soup in a bowl she sets
on the low table beside the sofa and does not say anything about eating it.

The specific sensations that can be written: a sore throat that makes
swallowing obvious. A stomach ache that comes in waves. A headache that
makes light uncomfortable. The particular tiredness where the body does not
want to move at all.

What not to write: "Emma had a cold." "Taro was ill with a stomach bug."
Show it through what the character notices, what they cannot do, what Gran
does. The child always recovers within the story or between stories; this
is not a tragedy setting.

**Appears in:** Emma sick (throat, cold, ear), Taro sick (stomach,
headache), recovery, first day well again.

## World Topology

The locations have a fixed spatial relationship. Stories should use this
consistently — not as exposition, but as background fact that surfaces
naturally.

```
             hill (day trip)
                 |
         Gran's field
          /          \
       The Oak      [open]
          \
        The Pond ———— bench (by willow)
             |
        Meadow path
        /           \
Gran's gate       The School
     |
Gran's cottage
     |
 Village lane
     |
  The Village
 (baker / post box / side street)
      |
  Vet + Doctor (side street)
```

Cross-references that should be used:
- From the top of the village lane, looking back: Gran's chimney, the line
  of the oak at the field's edge.
- From the hilltop (story 30): Gran's cottage very small, the oak a dark
  shape in the fields.
- The school bell can be heard from the meadow path on still mornings.
- The baker's smell reaches the village lane before you reach the door.
- The pond is visible from the meadow path once you pass the hedge gap.
- From the pond bench, the oak is just visible across the field.

These are not facts characters recite. They are things characters notice
while moving through the world.

## Seasonal Continuity

The oak, the pond, the meadow path, and Gran's garden change with the seasons.
Stories reference the same physical features across seasons to build a
persistent world. Examples:

- The squirrel's hole at the oak base appears in the autumn acorn story, is
  buried under snow in the winter story, and Biscuit sniffs at it in spring.
- The robin's nest in the oak fork is empty in autumn, built in spring, holds
  eggs in early summer.
- The pond is clear and full of frogs in summer; iced at the edges in winter.
  Fish are visible from the flat stone on still days; invisible when the water
  is cloudy or the surface is broken.
- Gran's apple tree is in blossom in spring, heavy with fruit in autumn.
  Windfalls begin in late summer.
- The pear tree has a single ripe week in early autumn. Before that the pears
  are hard; after that they are mealy or fallen.
- The vegetable patch is bare and dug over in late autumn and winter; seeds
  go in in spring; harvest runs from summer through autumn.
- The village lane is pleasant in summer and spring, cold and shadowed in
  winter; the puddle dip is worst in November and March.
- The pond bench is used most in summer and early autumn; in winter it is
  too cold and often damp; Gran wipes it with her sleeve before she sits.

## Writing Rules

### No source language
Each language version of a story is written independently. There is no
original. The story specification (SEED, LOCATION, CHARACTERS, KEY WORDS)
describes what happens — who does what, in what place, with what objects.
Each language takes that specification and writes the scene as if a fluent
native author were writing it for the first time.

German prose is not English prose with German words. Japanese prose is not
English prose in Japanese grammar. The sentence structure, the rhythm, the
things that get said aloud versus shown through action — all of these differ
by language and must feel native.

Examples of what this means in practice:
- Japanese will use shorter, more fragmented sentences than English.
- German can use compound words and longer clauses where English would use
  two short sentences.
- Chinese will be denser and may omit subjects where they are implied.
- None of these is a defect. They are the correct register for each language.

The only things that must match across all four versions:
- The same characters appear.
- The same place and season.
- The same event happens (Biscuit jumps in, the egg is found, the dough
  rises, etc.).
- Arithmetic answers, if present, are identical.
- Character names match the language-specific cast table.

### Reading level
Grade 1–2. Short sentences. Concrete actions. A child of seven should be
able to follow every sentence without explanation.

### Prose style: Beatrix Potter, not textbook
The world teaches itself through what happens, not through definitions.
Characters act and notice things. Objects appear through touch, smell, sound,
and weight — not through labels.

RIGHT:
  She turned it in her fingers. The cap was rough. The bottom was smooth.
  Biscuit pressed his nose into the hole and sneezed.
  The soup smelled of onion and something warm she couldn't name.

WRONG:
  "This is an acorn," said Gran. "It is small and round."
  Emma said, "I am holding an acorn."
  Taro learned that acorns come from oak trees.

### Pronouns
Use pronouns freely when the referent is clear and local. "She turned it in
her fingers" is correct because the reader knows who she is and what it is.
Do not use pronouns when the referent has not been introduced in the last
two sentences.

### Dialogue
Natural, brief, purposeful. People speak the way they actually speak to
children — not explaining, but reacting, pointing, asking. Gran does not
deliver information; she answers a question while doing something else.

RIGHT:  "Bury it," Gran said, without looking up from the weeds.
WRONG:  "That is an acorn," Gran explained. "If you plant it, it will grow
         into an oak tree just like this one."

### Sentence variety
Mix very short sentences with longer ones. Short sentences land facts and
actions. Longer sentences carry motion or atmosphere.

  The acorn was small. It fit inside Emma's fist.
  Biscuit ran ahead and came back and ran ahead again, nose low to the ground.

### Length
150–220 words per story. This is not a word count target — write the scene
fully and stop when it is done. Do not pad. Do not summarise at the end.

### What not to do
- No moral at the end.
- No "and they all went home happy."
- No character announcing what they have learned.
- No textbook constructions in any language (私はビルです / I am a boy / Das ist ein Apfel).
- No exposition about the world that a child in the scene would already know.

## Extended Cast (stories 196+)

### Mei
A girl, same age as Emma. She lives two lanes over from Gran's cottage — close enough
that she appears at the garden gate or the meadow path without much explanation, and
close enough that her cat Dou turns up at Gran's garden wall on her own. Mei's
grandmother Yun runs the Saturday market stall. Mei is quiet and precise: she measures
things before she touches them, finds the right place for things, notices immediately
when something is off-level or out of order. She knows the mill well — which board to
step over, where to stand so the flour dust doesn't get in your eyes. In the village
she is confident and purposeful. At the oak or the pond she is still learning the ground.

Language names: EN=Mei, DE=Mei, JP=メイ, ZH=梅

### Dou
Mei's cat. Small, grey, with one white ear and a tail she carries very upright. She
moves along walls and fences rather than through open ground, and appears in Gran's
garden without explanation, as cats do. She tolerates Biscuit — who has decided she
is beneath his notice — and makes Bello quietly miserable with her indifference. She
climbs the apple tree without effort and sits in the fork where no one can reach her.
She turns up without warning and leaves the same way. She is not unfriendly; she is
simply on her own schedule.

Language names: EN=Dou, DE=Dou, JP=ドウ, ZH=豆

### Yun
Mei's grandmother. She runs the market stall every Saturday — vegetables, eggs, dried
herbs in small paper bags sealed with a twist. She weighs things on a small brass
scale, makes change without hesitation, and knows the name of every regular customer.
She speaks to children the same way she speaks to adults: directly, without adjustment
downward. Her hands are fast and never idle. The stall table is always tidy; she
straightens it between customers without appearing to think about it.

Language names: EN=Yun, DE=Yun, JP=ユン, ZH=云

### Riku
The postman. He has a red bicycle and a large canvas bag that leans to one side when
it is full. He calls everyone by name when he hands something over, including the dogs
by name. He knows which houses get letters on which days, who is expecting a parcel,
who has been waiting. He is cheerful without being loud. His bicycle is always leaning
against something it perhaps should not be.

Language names: EN=Riku, DE=Riku, JP=陸, ZH=陆

### Stefan
The village police officer. He walks his beat slowly, hands clasped behind his back.
He nods at people he passes and sometimes stops to exchange a few words, but never for
long. He knows every child in the village by name and every dog by name. He has a
bicycle too, which he sometimes rides and sometimes pushes, apparently by preference.
His badge catches the sun on clear mornings. His presence on the main street means
everything is as it should be.

Language names: EN=Stefan, DE=Stefan, JP=ステファン, ZH=斯蒂芬

### Hana
The baker. She is at work before anyone else in the village is awake — the bakery
light is on when the lane is still dark. Her loaves are different sizes; she lines
them up in the window in order, big ones at the back, small ones at the front. Her
apron always has flour on it, even late in the afternoon. End-of-day loaves go at
half price and are still warm. The bakery smell reaches the bottom of Gran's lane
before you get to the door.

Language names: EN=Hana, DE=Hana, JP=ハナ, ZH=花

### Owen
The farmer whose field borders the meadow path on the west side. He keeps a flock
of sheep — fifteen or twenty; Emma has never been certain of the exact number. He
leans on gates in the manner of someone who has been doing it for a long time and
finds it productive. He has very big hands, a flat cap, and a sheepdog who works
the field beside him. He rarely speaks unless there is something worth saying. When
he does, it is usually about weather or animals and it is usually right.

Language names: EN=Owen, DE=Owen, JP=オーウェン, ZH=欧文

### Clara
The schoolteacher — already described in location 5, named here for reference. She
writes in large, careful letters on the blackboard and erases them completely before
writing the next thing. She keeps the nature table stocked. She calls on the quiet
children as readily as the ones with their hands up. Patient but not soft.

Language names: EN=Clara, DE=Klara, JP=クララ, ZH=克拉拉

### Vern
An old carpenter with a workshop on the side street, between the vet and the doctor's
surgery. He is spare with words and generous with time — if a child brings him
something broken, he examines it properly before deciding. His workshop smells of
sawdust and linseed oil. He has names for every tool and uses them as habit, not
instruction — "hand me the spokeshave" as if of course you know what that is. When
he is working, the sounds come in sequence: measuring, marking, cutting, planing,
fitting. He fits a joint without glue first, takes it apart, notes what needs
correcting. He has been doing this long enough that his hands know what his eyes
have already told them.

Language names: EN=Vern, DE=Vern, JP=ヴェルン, ZH=弗恩

**Named updates to existing cast:**
- The vet (location 10) is **Dr. Lena**.
- The doctor (location 11) is **Dr. Anand**.

## Additional Locations (stories 196+)

### 13. The Mill
A small stone building at the edge of the village where a stream has been diverted
to run beneath it. The water wheel is on the east side — large, dark with damp,
turning slowly when the sluice is open. Inside, the floor is stone and the air is
always white with flour dust. The millstones are very heavy, flat-faced, turning
against each other with a low grinding sound you feel through the floor before you
hear it with your ears. Grain goes in at the top through a wooden hopper; flour
comes out below, fine and warm, into a cloth sack. The ceiling beams are low.
Everything has a fine white coating.

The stream that feeds the wheel runs out the other side of the building, slower
and quieter, into a stone channel and then into the ditch along the lane. In dry
summers the stream drops and the wheel turns slower; after heavy rain it races.

Mei knows the building well. She knows which floorboard to step over, where to
stand so the flour dust does not get in your eyes, how to open the sluice lever
and what happens to the wheel when you do.

**Appears in:** grinding grain, flour and dust, the wheel under different
conditions (fast/slow/stopped), the stream in rain vs dry weather, Mei explaining
machinery, the weight of the millstones, things that turn and grind, the sound
felt through the floor.

### 14. Vern's Workshop
On the side street, between the vet and the doctor's surgery, behind a low door
that stands open in good weather. One long room, a very large workbench along the
back wall with a vise at one end, tools hung above in size order on wooden pegs.
The floor has a permanent layer of shavings and sawdust that Vern sweeps to the
corner but never entirely removes. It smells of sawdust and linseed oil and
something resinous from the green wood stacked along the side wall.

Different woods are stored separately: darker ones against the far wall, pale
fresh-cut ones near the door to dry. Vern knows each by touch and smell without
looking. The vise holds things still while he works. Each tool has a specific peg;
he notices immediately if one is wrong or missing.

**Appears in:** Vern making something, a broken object brought in for repair,
identifying wood by touch and smell, measuring and fitting, tool names, the vise,
a joint fitted without glue, children watching Vern work, the sequence of sounds
when carpentry is done correctly.

### 15. The Hill
A long slope at the far end of Gran's field — not steep but high enough that from
the top the cottage looks very small and the oak is a dark shape at the field's
edge. Owen's sheep graze the upper slope and move slowly across it over the
course of a morning.

From the top on a clear day: the village below, the mill chimney, the lane. To
the west, clouds build first. Gran says weather comes from the west and you can
see it coming if you go up early enough. The grass here is shorter, cropped close.
The wind comes without the trees to break it. In winter the hilltop is the coldest
place in the valley. In summer on still days it is the best view, and sounds from
the village carry clearly upward.

A gate at the very top leads to the next valley — another farm, not in the
stories, but the gate is there and sometimes open.

**Appears in:** wide view, weather coming in from the west, Owen's sheep on the
slope, the village seen from above, the cottage very small below, wind without
shelter, distance and sound (things loud close, faint from the hill), the mill
chimney visible, the gate to somewhere else.

### 16. The Root Cellar
Below Gran's cottage, reached by a wooden door set into the ground at an angle
near the kitchen wall. The door is heavier than it looks; it must be propped open
with an iron bar or it falls. Five stone steps, worn and steeper than indoor
stairs. The room below is cool even in summer, cold in winter, and dark until
you bring a lamp.

It smells of earth and stored things: apples in crates along one wall (the sweet,
slightly fermented smell of ones that are turning), onions hanging in ropes from
the ceiling beam, turnips in a wooden box, potatoes in a hessian sack that Gran
checks for rot. Shelves hold jars: preserved plums, pickled cabbage, dried beans,
each labelled in Gran's careful writing.

In summer the root cellar is the coolest place on the property — the floor is
earth, the walls are stone, and the temperature does not follow the weather above.
In winter it does not freeze. The lamp Gran uses is an old metal one, lit with a
match. The shadows it throws are large and move when the flame moves.

**Appears in:** fetching apples or vegetables, checking for rot, the smell of
stored things, cool in summer heat, jars of preserved food, the heavy door, the
lamp and its shadows, counting and sorting stored vegetables, what is left at the
end of winter.
