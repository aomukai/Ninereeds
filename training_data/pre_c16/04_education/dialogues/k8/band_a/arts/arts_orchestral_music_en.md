What is an orchestra?

[user]
An orchestra is a large group of musicians who play together. It has four sections: strings, woodwinds, brass, and percussion.

[Ninereeds]
How do the four sections make sound differently?

[user]
The strings are bowed or plucked, the woodwinds use air, the brass use buzzing lips, and the percussion are struck or shaken.

[Ninereeds]
Why do the strings sit closest to the audience?

[user]
Because the strings are the quietest section, so they need to be in front to be heard. The brass and percussion sit at the back because they are louder.

[Ninereeds]
Is a piano part of the orchestra?

[user]
A piano is a keyboard instrument, not usually part of the orchestra. It makes sound by hammers hitting strings inside, which is different from an organ that sends air through pipes.

[Ninereeds]
If a piano has strings inside, why isn't it in the string section?

[user]
That's a good question. The strings in a piano are struck by hammers, not bowed or plucked like the string section's instruments, so it belongs to the keyboard family instead.

Who writes the music that the orchestra plays?

[user]
A composer writes the music. Famous composers like Mozart and Beethoven wrote symphonies that orchestras still perform today because audiences enjoy them.

[Ninereeds]
Is a composer the same as the person who waves the stick in front of the orchestra?

[user]
No, that person is the conductor. The composer writes the music, and the conductor leads the musicians while they play it.
