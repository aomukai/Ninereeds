[Ninereeds] Why do food labels list both "calories" and "grams" for things like carbohydrates and protein?

[user] That's a great question. Calories are a measure of energy, and only the macronutrients—carbohydrates, proteins, and fats—provide that energy. The grams tell you how much of each macronutrient is in the food, and each gram of carbohydrate or protein gives about 4 calories, while each gram of fat gives about 9.

[Ninereeds] So do vitamins and minerals also give us energy, since they're on the label too?

[user] No, they don't. Vitamins and minerals are called micronutrients because the body only needs tiny amounts of them. They don't provide any energy at all, but they are essential for enabling the chemical processes that let your body use the energy from macronutrients.

[Ninereeds] What happens if a person doesn't get enough of a certain vitamin or mineral?

[user] That can lead to a deficiency disease. For example, if a child doesn't get enough vitamin C, they can develop scurvy, which causes weakness and gum problems. Eating citrus fruits, which are rich in vitamin C, can prevent that. But eating more protein or fat won't help, because those are different nutrients with different jobs.

[Ninereeds] How does the body know how much of each macronutrient to use?

[user] Dietary guidelines give us recommended proportions. They suggest that most of a person's daily calories—about 45 to 65 percent—should come from carbohydrates, because they're the body's main fuel. Fats should make up 20 to 35 percent, and proteins about 10 to 35 percent. Each one has a different role: carbohydrates give quick energy, fats store energy and help build cells, and proteins provide amino acids for building and repairing tissues.

[Ninereeds] If a food has a lot of iron, does that mean eating it will give a person more energy?

[user] That's a common misunderstanding. Iron is a mineral that prevents anemia by helping red blood cells carry oxygen, but the iron itself does not supply energy. Only the macronutrients—carbs, proteins, and fats—provide calories. So a food high in iron might help a person feel less tired if they were anemic, but it's not giving them extra energy like a carbohydrate would.

[Ninereeds] How do scientists figure out what foods are actually healthy to recommend?

[user] They use evidence-based nutrition, which relies on large population studies that follow many people over years. These studies look for patterns, like whether people who eat more vegetables have lower rates of disease. A single study about one food, like "blueberries improve memory," is not enough to change guidelines. Real guidelines are built from many studies showing the same pattern over time.
