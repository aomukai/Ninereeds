Why do some countries hold elections but still not seem like true democracies?  

[user]  
That’s a great question. In a true democracy, power is spread out through checks and balances so no single leader can control everything. But in authoritarianism, a leader concentrates power and suppresses opposition, even if elections are held. Elections alone don’t guarantee democracy—you also need rule of law, a free press, and a strong civil society.

What makes it hard for people to know what’s true in a democracy?

One big challenge is disinformation—false information spread deliberately to mislead voters. When people can’t tell what’s true, it becomes harder to make informed decisions and engage in civic life. How does a free press help keep democracy strong?  

[user]  
A free press holds leaders accountable by reporting independently on what they do. In authoritarian regimes, the government controls or censors the media, so citizens don’t get the full picture. But in a democracy, journalists can investigate and share facts, which helps voters make good choices and keeps power in check.

What else can citizens do besides voting to help democracy?

Civic engagement includes many actions: attending community meetings, organizing with neighbors, writing to leaders, and debating issues respectfully. Voting is important, but democracy needs people to stay involved between elections too. That ongoing participation ensures citizens have a voice in decisions and can hold leaders accountable.

Why are some new democracies fragile and at risk of falling apart?  

[user]  
Fragile democracies often lack strong rule of law or a robust civil society—the networks of groups and organizations that help people work together. Without those foundations, it’s easier for leaders to weaken institutions and push the country back toward authoritarianism. Public trust also tends to be low, which makes it harder to solve problems together.

Has democracy spread to every country in the world?  

[user]  
Global democracy has expanded a lot since the 20th century, but it hasn’t reached everywhere. Many new democracies remain fragile because their institutions are weak and public trust is low. So while more people live under democratic systems than ever before, the health of those democracies still depends on strong rule of law, free press, and active citizens.
