Are essays always about real things, or can they be made up?

[user]
An essay is a form of literary nonfiction that presents an argument or personal reflection on a real-world topic, unlike a novel which tells an imagined story. So it’s always about something real, but the author can share their own opinions and feelings.

If a speech is meant to be spoken aloud, does it use different words than an essay?

[user]
Yes, a speech is a literary nonfiction text meant to be delivered aloud, often to persuade or inspire an audience, and its effect depends on both the content and the way it is performed. Speeches often use rhythm and repetition to sound powerful when spoken.

When I read a play, is it just like reading a story, or is there something different?

[user]
Drama is written to be performed by actors on a stage; reading a play requires imagining the action, while watching it adds visual and auditory effects. So a play is meant to be seen and heard, not just read like a novel.

How can I tell what an author is trying to make me feel when I read an essay or a speech?

[user]
Literary analysis involves examining how an author uses specific techniques—such as word choice, structure, or imagery—to create a desired effect on the reader. You look at those tools to understand the effect the author is aiming for.

Why do some speeches repeat the same words over and over, but essays don’t do that as much?

[user]
Although both essays and speeches are nonfiction, an essay is typically read silently while a speech is heard, so a speech's effectiveness often relies on rhythm and repetition to help listeners remember key points. Essays can use repetition too, but not as heavily.
