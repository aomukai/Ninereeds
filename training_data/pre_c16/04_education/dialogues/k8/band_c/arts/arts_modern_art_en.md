Matisse believed that color itself could carry feeling. Warm colors like red and orange feel energetic, while cool colors like blue and green feel calm. By choosing green for a face, he wanted to convey a mood—perhaps something thoughtful or strange—rather than a realistic skin tone.

Exactly. Cubism, practiced by Picasso, broke objects into geometric shapes and showed several viewpoints in one painting. In *Les Demoiselles d’Avignon*, the women’s faces are shown from multiple angles at once, so you see the front and side of a nose in the same image—it’s like walking around the subject while staying still.

But wouldn’t that make the painting confusing instead of beautiful?  

[user]  
For some viewers it is confusing at first, but Picasso wanted to challenge the idea that a painting must show just one frozen moment. By fragmenting perspective, he gave the brain more information about the subject’s shape and space. It’s a different kind of beauty—more like a puzzle than a photograph.

Yes, that’s Salvador Dalí’s *The Persistence of Memory*. Surrealism explored the unconscious mind—dreams, memories, and irrational thoughts. A melting clock represents how time feels soft and stretchy in a dream, not rigid like in waking life. That’s Abstract Expressionism, and Jackson Pollock’s drip paintings are a great example. People often think they’re completely random splashes, but Pollock controlled the flow of paint by moving his whole body over the canvas, choosing where the drips landed. He used rhythm and layering to express emotion—chaotic on the surface, yet deliberate underneath.  

[Ninereeds]  
If he controlled it, why didn’t he paint something recognizable?  

[user]  
Abstract Expressionists believed that a painting didn’t need a recognizable object to hold meaning. The gesture itself—the energy of the movement, the thickness of the paint—could directly communicate feelings like excitement, anger, or calm. It’s emotion made visible without a story or a person in the picture.
