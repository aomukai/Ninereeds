Then an old woman walked past. She saw the child frowning at the paper. The old woman said, “Let me see.” The child handed her the note. The old woman looked at the words. “Some of these are hard to read,” she said. “The writing is not neat. That makes the message illusory — it looks like words, but the meaning is hard to catch.”

The child nodded. She took a clean piece of paper and wrote carefully, making each letter clear and distinct. This time, the old woman could read it easily. “Now the message is intelligible,” the old woman said. “Because the words are neat, the meaning is not lost. It is not incorrect or illogical — it is clear.”

The child smiled. She ran to the millpond and gave the note to the old lady there. The old lady read it and said, “Ah, the cat is in the barn. Thank you for telling me.” Then she found the cat and gave it some milk.

The child learned that intelligible matters because when words are clear, the message gets through. If the writing is messy, the meaning is immaterial — it might as well not exist. But when it is intelligible, people can understand and help each other.
