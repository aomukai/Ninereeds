The child did not know which way to go. So she picked up a leaf from the ground. Then she closed her eyes and let the leaf fall. The leaf landed pointing left. So the child went left. That was an arbitrary choice. It did not matter which way she chose because both paths led to the same barn. Later, she saw an old woman sitting on a doorstep. The old woman was sewing a quilt. The child asked, “Why did you choose that color?” The woman said, “I flipped a stone to decide.” The child thought about this. The woman’s choice was arbitrary too. She did not look at the stone. She just let it fall. Then the child noticed a small stone in her shoe, near her heel. She took it out. The stone had a mark that looked like a kiss. That was just a shape. It did not imply anything. The old woman smiled and said, “An arbitrary choice is acceptable when both options are the same.” The child nodded. The old woman was an expert at making quilts. She had passed many exams in sewing, but she still used a stone to pick colors. That seemed strange. Then the child understood: arbitrary matters because it lets you decide when you have no reason. You can flip a leaf or pick left without looking. It moves you forward. That is why arbitrary matters.
