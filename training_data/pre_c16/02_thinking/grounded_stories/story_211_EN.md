Yun came through the mill door with a sack over each shoulder.

"This one is wheat," she said, setting the first down. The sack was pale brown and full of soft weight. "This one is barley." The second sack was darker, smaller, heavier for its size.

Gran opened the wheat sack first. She scooped out a handful and let it run through her fingers into the hopper. The grains were golden, round, small enough to fall like rain.

She opened the barley sack. The grains were longer, paler, with a sharp point at one end.

"They look different," Gran said. She turned the wheel. The stones began to turn, low and deep, a sound you felt in your chest before you heard it.

The wheat went through first. Flour came out the bottom — light, almost white, so fine it floated in the air. Gran caught it in a cloth sack.

Then the barley. The flour was darker, greyish, and when Gran rubbed a pinch between her fingers it felt rough, not smooth.

"Feel," she said, and held out her hand.

Yun touched the wheat flour, then the barley flour. "Different," she said.

"Different," Gran agreed. She filled one jar with the light flour and one with the dark. Both jars sat side by side on the shelf, full, waiting.
