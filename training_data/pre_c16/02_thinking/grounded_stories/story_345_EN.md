The cloth was soft and white. Yun wiped the brass pan first — slow circles, inside and out. The metal gleamed under her fingers.

Mei watched. The stall was empty now. No vegetables. No eggs. No paper bags twisted shut. Just the flat wooden table and the brass scale in the middle of it.

Yun wiped the arm next, where the numbers were stamped into the brass. She wiped the base. She folded the cloth and set it aside.

“The box,” she said.

Mei lifted the lid. It was a wooden box lined with dark felt, and it fitted the scale exactly — there was a hollow for the pan, a groove for the arm, a space at the bottom for the weights.

Yun lifted the scale with both hands. She lowered it into the box. It settled without a sound.

Mei pressed the lid down. The catch clicked.

“It is quiet now,” Mei said.

Yun nodded. She slid the box under the stall table, where it would sit until Saturday came again. The market was done. The street was quiet. The only sound was the cloth being folded once more and put away.
