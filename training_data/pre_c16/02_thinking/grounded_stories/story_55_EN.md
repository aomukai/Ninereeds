The morning was cold. Gran took the stick from beside the shed and hooked it under the compost lid. The wood creaked. A puff of steam rose into the air.  

Emma leaned closer. The compost was dark and crumbly, with bits of carrot peel and eggshell mixed in. It smelled like wet earth and something warmer underneath—like bread left out in the rain.  

Gran pointed. A fat earthworm wriggled between the layers, its body glistening. "Turning scraps into soil," she said, and nudged a clump with the stick. The rot underneath was soft, almost black.  

Biscuit pushed forward, sniffing hard. His nose twitched. Then—*ah-CHOO!* He jerked back, shaking his head. Emma laughed.  

Gran let the lid drop. The steam curled once more, then vanished. "Next month," she said, "this will be ready for the beans." She wiped her hands on her apron and walked toward the vegetable patch. Biscuit sneezed again, trotting after her.  

Emma stayed a moment longer, looking at the closed bin. The smell still hung in the air—warm and alive, like the garden breathing.
