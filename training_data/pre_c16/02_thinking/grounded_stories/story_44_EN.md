Gran lifted the lid of the bucket and sighed. “No fish,” she said. “Supper will be thin tonight.” Taro leaned over to look. The bucket was empty, the bottom dry and dusty. Biscuit sniffed at it and sneezed.

Taro grabbed the fishing rod from the garden shed. “I’ll get some,” he said. He ran down the meadow path, Biscuit trotting beside him. The pond was still, the water reflecting the willow branches. Taro sat on the flat stone and cast the line. Biscuit lay down beside him, ears pricked, watching the ripples. He didn’t bark or chase butterflies. He just watched.

The line twitched. Taro pulled it in carefully. A small fish wriggled on the hook. He dropped it into the bucket with a splash. Biscuit stood up, sniffed the bucket, and lay down again.

The line twitched twice more. Each time, Taro pulled in another fish. The bucket was full now, the water sloshing as the fish swam in circles.

“None to start with, three now,” Taro said. “That is what Gran needs.” He packed up the rod and carried the bucket carefully. Biscuit walked beside him, nose close to the sloshing water. Taro counted aloud, “One, two, three. Right.”
