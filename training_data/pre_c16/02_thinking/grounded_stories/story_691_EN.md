Mei stood at the oak and looked up.

The branches spread wide and dark against the spring sky. The trunk was very thick — thicker than her arms could reach around. She put her hand flat on the bark. It was rough and deep, like old stone. Not like the trees on her street at all.

She traced a line with her finger. The bark went deeper than her fingernail could follow.

“There’s a hole,” Emma said.

She pointed at the base, between two big roots. Mei knelt and looked. The hole went back into the dark. A few dry leaves were pushed into the opening. The ground around it was worn smooth.

“A squirrel lives there,” Emma said.

Mei touched the edge of the hole with one finger. It was cool and dry.

Dou sat on a root a little way off. She lifted one paw and washed it slowly, then the other. She did not look at the oak or the hole. She did not look at anything.

Then she stood up and walked away. No sound. No hurry. She was simply gone between one breath and the next.

Mei put her hand on the bark again. It felt different here. Bigger. Older. The leaves overhead were small and new, and the light under the tree was quiet and green.
