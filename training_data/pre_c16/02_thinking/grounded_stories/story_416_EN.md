Gran lit the lamp. The flame stood still for a moment, then settled.

“Take it down,” she said. “I need to know how many jars of plums are left.”

Emma took the lamp by its wire handle. The metal was warm.

The door was heavy. She propped it open with the iron bar the way Gran did. The steps went down steep, worn in the middle, darker with each step.

The flame shook. Her hand was not quite steady. The shadows on the stone walls moved with the flame — larger than they should be, sliding over the jars on the shelves.

She reached the bottom. The floor was earth, hard-packed and cool under her shoes. The smell was thick: apples turning sweet, onion ropes, the deep cold smell of stone and soil.

She counted the jars on the shelf. Six. She said it aloud to herself.

Then she heard something. A shift. A scrape. From the dark corner behind the stair.

She looked. The lamp threw the shadow of the stair across the wall. The flame flickered. The shadow moved.

Nothing there.

She stood still. The flame went still too. The sound did not come again.

She turned and went back up the steps, holding the lamp very straight.
