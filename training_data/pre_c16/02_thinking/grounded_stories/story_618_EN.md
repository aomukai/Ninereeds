Owen leaned on the gate where the meadow path met his field.

Emma stopped beside him. The gate was wood, grey and smooth at the top where hands had rested. Beyond it, the field sloped upward, grass short and even.

She counted the sheep. Seventeen. Then two moved from behind a bush, and it was nineteen.

Owen said nothing. He looked across the field the way Gran looked at the garden — not at one thing, but at everything at once. His hands rested on the top bar of the gate. One thumb moved, then stopped.

The grass rustled. A sheep lifted its head, looked at them, put its head down again.

Emma leaned on the gate too. The wood was warm from the sun. She could smell the sheep — not a clean smell, but not a bad one. Grass and wool and something damp.

"Do they know you?" she asked.

Owen did not answer for a moment. Then he said, "They know the gate."

One sheep began to walk toward them. It stopped a few paces away, close enough that Emma could see the grain of its wool, the dark slit of its eye.

Owen waited.

The sheep took another step. Then it turned and walked back into the field.
