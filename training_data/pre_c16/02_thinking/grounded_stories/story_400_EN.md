Gran lifted the heavy cellar door. Cold air came up from the dark.

“I need apples,” she said. “Take the lamp.”

Taro picked up the metal lamp. It swung from his hand, clanking softly. He followed Gran to the top of the stone steps. She struck a match and lit the wick. The flame stood small and yellow.

“You carry it down,” she said.

Taro took the lamp by the wire handle. The steps were steep and worn. He went slowly. The flame wobbled with each step.

Halfway down, the match went out in his fingers. He stopped. The dark pressed in close. He fumbled for a new match from the box in his pocket. His fingers were cold. The match slipped. It fell. He heard it hit the stone floor below.

“Gran,” he said.

“Pick it up.”

He went down the last steps in the dark. His hand found the match on the cold stone. It was damp. Useless.

Gran came down the steps behind him. She held out another match.

“Try again,” she said. “This time, hold it steady.”

Taro stood still. He struck the match against the box. The spark caught. The flame burned bright and clean. He touched it to the wick.

The lamp lit. The cellar came back — crates, jars, ropes of onions. He held the light very carefully.
