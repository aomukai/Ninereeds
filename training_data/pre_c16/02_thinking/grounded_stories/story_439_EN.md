Gran lifted the iron bar. The cellar door opened with its usual heavy sound. Cold air rose from the steps.

“Potatoes,” she said. “Four will do.”

Emma took the lamp. The glass was warm in her hand. She went down the five stone steps without holding the wall.

The root cellar smelled of earth and apples and the sweet, turning smell of stored things. She held the lamp up. The shadows jumped across the walls — the ropes of onions, the crates, the jars on the shelves. She did not stop to look at them.

The potato sack stood against the far wall. She knelt on the cold floor and untied the string at the top. Her fingers went in. The potatoes were firm and dusty.

She counted them. Twelve.

She picked four. Two in each hand. Then she tied the sack again and stood up.

The lamp threw her shadow against the cellar wall. It was tall and moved when she moved. Emma looked at it and did not stop. She knew the steps now. She knew the door at the top.

She came up into the kitchen. The light was bright. Gran was at the table, rolling dough.

Emma set the potatoes on the wooden board.

“Four,” she said.

Gran nodded. She did not say anything else.
