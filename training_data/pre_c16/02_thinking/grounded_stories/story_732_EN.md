Gran found the nest first. She was weeding around the oak when she stopped and pointed up into the fork.

Emma stood on her toes. "I see it."

Three eggs inside the cup of moss and twigs. Small and blue. So blue that the sky looked pale beside them.

"Robin," Gran said, and went back to the weeds.

Taro climbed up to the low branch and leaned in. The robin flew off in a hurry and did not come back until he climbed down.

---

Every morning Emma checked. The robin sat. The eggs stayed the same.

Taro got bored of watching. He ran to the pond and came back with mud on his knees.

Then one morning the eggs were gone. Three open mouths instead. Big yellow triangles.

"Chicks," Emma said.

The mouths closed when the robin came. They opened again when she left.

---

The third week the mouths had feathers around them. Grey-brown and fluffed. One chick stood on the edge of the nest and flapped.

Taro saw it first. "It's going to fall."

It did not fall. It flew — badly, low, across the grass into the hedge.

The next day the nest was empty.

Emma stood under the oak and looked up. The fork held only air and the shape of last year's leaves.

"Gone," she said.

Taro nodded. "They grew."
