Vern turned the handle on the vise. The iron jaws opened, then closed. He slid a piece of oak between them and turned the handle again.

The wood held still.

Emma stood beside the workbench with her hands behind her back. The vise clamped the oak tight. She could not move it if she tried.

Vern picked up a plane. The blade was sharp. He set it at the edge of the wood and pushed. The plane moved. The wood did not move. A shaving curled up from the blade — thin and pale and warm-looking. It dropped onto the bench.

Vern pushed again. Another shaving. Same curl. Same drop.

Emma watched his hands. One held the plane steady. The other pressed at the front. He did not rush. The wood stayed where the vise held it.

She reached out and touched the shaving on the bench. It was smooth on one side and rough on the other. She lifted it. It curled around her finger.

Vern stopped pushing. He looked at the edge of the oak. Then he looked at her.

“Hold it up,” he said.

She held the shaving up. Light came through it.

Vern nodded once and turned back to the vise. His hand found the handle. He turned it again.
