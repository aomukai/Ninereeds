Mei pointed to the upper millstone. It was dark grey and very still.

Taro put both hands on it and pushed. Nothing happened. He put his shoulder against the stone and pushed harder. The stone did not move at all.

“It is too heavy,” he said.

Mei walked to the wall and lifted a long piece of wood. The lever was thicker than her arm. She carried it back and laid it on the floor.

“This is what they used,” she said. She showed him where the lever had been fitted under the stone. “One person could lift it. But not by hand.”

Taro looked at the stone again. It was wider than both their arms stretched out. It was thick as a step.

“How heavy is it?” he said.

Mei did not answer. She put her palm flat on the stone. The surface felt cold and smooth. Taro put his hand beside hers.

“It crushes everything underneath,” Mei said. “The grain. When it turns, the grain is crushed into flour.”

Taro looked at the gap between the two stones. It was very thin. He could not see how anything passed through.

The mill was quiet. The lever lay on the floor. The stone did not move.
