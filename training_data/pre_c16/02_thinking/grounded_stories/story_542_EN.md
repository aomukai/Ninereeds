Owen was at the gate. The sun was out, warm on Taro’s arms.

Owen’s coat was off. It hung over the top bar of the gate, sleeves empty, swinging a little.

Taro stopped. Biscuit stopped too, nose at the gatepost.

Owen nodded. “Afternoon.”

“Afternoon,” said Taro.

The same gate. The same lean — Owen’s elbows on the top bar, hands crossed. But different. In autumn the wood was wet. Owen wore his coat then, collar up, and the field behind him was tall and yellow.

Now the coat was off. The field was green. Fresh grass, short and even, like it had been cut that morning.

Owen looked out across the field. He did not say anything. He just stood there, waiting for something — maybe the sheep to move, maybe nothing.

Biscuit pushed his nose under the gate and sniffed the dry ground on the other side. Then he sat down, tongue out, waiting too.

Taro put his hand on the top bar. The wood was warm. Dry. Not the same as last time.

Owen looked at him. “Coming through?”

“Not today,” said Taro.

Owen nodded again. Taro walked on. Biscuit followed.
