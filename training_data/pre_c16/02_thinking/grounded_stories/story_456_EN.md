A dry leaf lay on the grass near the garden wall. It was brown and curled at the edges.

Then it moved.

Not blown across the ground in a rush. It lifted, turned once, and settled a few inches to the left.

Bello saw it.

He crouched. His front legs bent. His ears went forward — two small flags pointing at the leaf.

The leaf moved again. A shift, a slide, a little skip sideways.

Bello's tail went still.

Emma sat on the stone bench near the kitchen door. She had been watching the apple tree, but now she watched Bello. She knew what the leaf was doing. Wind under it. A gust too small to feel on her face, but enough to push a dry leaf across the grass.

Bello did not know that.

He took one slow step forward. Then another. His nose pointed at the leaf. His whole body was tight and ready.

The leaf rustled. It lifted, hung in the air for a moment, and dropped.

Bello sprang.

He landed where the leaf had been. The leaf was not there. It had skidded sideways on a new breath of wind. Bello scrambled, turned, crouched again.

Emma did not call him. She just watched.

The leaf skittered across the grass. Bello followed.
