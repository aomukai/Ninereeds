Emma carried the lamp down the stone steps. The light swung and the shadows moved.

In the back corner, behind the onion rope, a row of jars sat on the shelf. Most were full. One was small and low, pushed to the side.

She pulled it out. The glass was dusty. Inside, the jam was dark red — almost black. But it was the label she looked at.

The paper was curled at the edges. The ink was pale, like water that had dried and faded. She could see a curve, a line, a smudge. Not letters anymore.

"Gran," she said.

Gran came down the steps. She took the jar and tilted it toward the lamp. The light caught the paper.

"Strawberry," she said. "From last July."

Emma touched the label with her finger. "I can't read it."

"No," Gran said. "It sat here too long. The dark and the damp took the ink."

"How old is it?"

Gran looked at the jar again. "We picked those berries on a hot morning. The sun was strong. Your hands were sticky."

Emma tried to remember. She could not.

But the jar was heavy in her hands. The jam inside was still dark. Still red.
