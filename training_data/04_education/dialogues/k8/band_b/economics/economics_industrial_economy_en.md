Before the Industrial Revolution, how did people make things like clothes and tools?

[user]
Before the Industrial Revolution, most goods were made by hand by individual craftspeople in small workshops or at home. A person might spin thread on a spinning wheel, and a weaver would turn it into cloth on a hand loom, one piece at a time.

[Ninereeds]
So what changed that made people start building big factories instead?

[user]
The big change was the steam engine. A steam engine could power many machines at once, so instead of one person working a hand tool, a factory could have dozens of machines all running together, making goods much faster than any craftsperson could.

[Ninereeds]
But who had the money to build a factory and buy all those machines?

[user]
That's where capital comes in. Capital is money or things like buildings and machines that are used to produce goods. An entrepreneur—a person who starts a business—would need a lot of capital to build a factory and buy steam engines and looms. So capital became just as important as land for making things.

[Ninereeds]
Were the people who worked in those factories treated well?

[user]
Factory workers often faced dangerous conditions. They worked very long hours, sometimes 12 to 16 hours a day, and the machines were unsafe—a child could get hurt easily. Wages were very low, and there were no rules to protect workers.

[Ninereeds]
So what did the workers do to try to make things better?

[user]
Workers started to form unions. A union is a group of workers who join together to demand better wages, shorter hours, and safer workplaces. By speaking as one group, they had more power to negotiate with the factory owners.

[Ninereeds]
How did railroads help factories grow even more?

[user]
Railroads allowed factories to ship raw materials, like cotton or iron, from far away, and then send finished goods, like cloth or tools, to cities all across the country. This connected industrial cities and created much bigger markets for the things factories made.
