Why do people talk about GDP so much when they describe how a country is doing?  

[user]  
GDP measures the total value of all goods and services produced in a country in a year, but it doesn't tell us how that money is shared among the people. A country can have a high GDP and still have many citizens who are not well-off.  

[Ninereeds]  
If GDP doesn't show how money is shared, how do economists measure economic inequality?  

[user]  
They use a tool called the Gini coefficient. It puts a number between 0 and 1 — 0 means everyone has the same income, and 1 means one person has all the income. Countries like South Africa have a high Gini, while Nordic countries have a lower one.

Why do some multinational corporations choose to build factories in poorer countries?  

[user]  
Multinational corporations often move production to countries with lower labor costs so they can make higher profits. This can create jobs for local workers, but the jobs may pay low wages, and the corporation may avoid paying taxes in the host country, which can widen inequality there.  

[Ninereeds]  
If making profits in other countries can sometimes hurt people, what makes some countries grow faster than others anyway?  

[user]  
That’s what development economics studies. It turns out that factors like strong education systems, good infrastructure, and honest institutions matter more than just having natural resources. Countries that invest in these things tend to grow faster over time.

How does borrowing money help a country or a business grow, instead of just causing debt?  

[user]  
Credit allows people and businesses to borrow money for productive investments — like building a factory or buying better machines. That investment can create more goods and jobs, which boost the economy. But if too much debt is taken on and borrowers can’t repay, it can trigger a financial crisis.

Globalization connects countries through trade and investment — so why don’t all countries benefit equally from it?  

[user]  
Globalization has raised living standards in many places, but its benefits are spread unevenly. Some regions grow a lot, while others get left behind, and within countries the gap between rich and poor can actually get bigger. That’s why economic inequality is such a key issue in the modern global economy.
