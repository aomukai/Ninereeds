Music historians divide Western classical music into three main eras that happened in order: the Baroque period (about 1600 to 1750), then the Classical period (about 1750 to 1820), and finally the Romantic period (about 1820 to 1900). So composers like Bach came before Mozart, and Mozart came before Beethoven.

[Ninereeds] What made each period sound different from the others?

[user] Baroque composers like Bach and Handel used a technique called counterpoint, where two or more independent melodies weave together at the same time. In the Classical period, Mozart and Haydn preferred a single clear melody with simpler accompaniment, which made their music feel more balanced and orderly.

[Ninereeds] Did Baroque music have any special decorations, like fancy notes?

[user] Yes—Baroque music added many ornamental flourishes, such as trills and mordents, to decorate the main melody. Classical composers cut back on those decorations to keep the melody clean and easy to follow, while Romantic composers later brought back expressive ornaments, but this time tied to strong emotions.

[Ninereeds] How did Romantic composers like Beethoven show emotions so strongly?

[user] Romantic composers used dramatic changes in volume—going from very soft to very loud quickly—and wrote for much larger orchestras than before. That helped them create intense feelings like excitement, sadness, or triumph, which was a big shift from the more controlled and elegant style of the Classical period.

[Ninereeds] I've heard of Beethoven—is he from the Baroque period too, since his music is so old?

[user] That's a common mix-up. Beethoven actually lived at the end of the Classical period and into the early Romantic period. His music uses big emotional contrasts and a large orchestra, not the complex counterpoint of Baroque music. So he's considered a Romantic composer, not a Baroque one.

[Ninereeds] So does all music from those periods sound pretty much the same to someone who hasn't studied it?

[user] Not at all. Baroque music, like Bach's fugues, has many layers of melody moving together. Classical music, like Mozart's symphonies, has clear, balanced melodies that are easy to hum. Romantic music, like Beethoven's symphonies, tells a story with dramatic ups and downs. People often say they can feel the difference right away once they know what to listen for.
