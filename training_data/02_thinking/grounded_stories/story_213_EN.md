The hopper sat at the top of the millstones. It was a wooden funnel, wide at the top, narrow at the bottom.

Emma picked up a single grain of wheat from the sack. She held it over the empty hopper and let go.

It fell straight down. It did not wobble or slow. It dropped into the dark, and a moment later there was a tiny tap as it hit the stone below.

Mei stood beside her. "It falls by itself," she said. "No one pushes it."

Taro came close and looked into the hopper. The bottom was a thin slit of darkness. "Where does it go?"

"Down," said Mei. "Gravity pulls it. Everything falls the same way."

Emma took another grain and dropped it. Same drop. Same tap. She did it again, and the sound was the same.

Taro moved to the chute below the stones. He held out his hand, palm up. Gran had run the stones empty the day before, but a fine dust of flour still clung to the wood. He felt nothing fall—then a single tiny fleck landed on his skin.

He looked at it. A speck of white on his palm.

"The grain falls into the stones," Mei said. "The flour falls onto your hand. The same way."

Emma put her hand beside Taro's. They waited. Nothing else came.

The mill was still. The hopper was empty. But the grain had fallen straight, and they had seen it.
