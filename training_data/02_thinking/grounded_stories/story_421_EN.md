The lamp flame shrank. It bent sideways. Then it was out.

Dark came down like a lid. Taro did not move. He could not see his own hand in front of his face. The air was cool and still and smelled of earth and apples. He held his breath and listened.

A step creaked overhead. Then another. Then Gran’s voice, not loud: “Taro.”

He did not answer. He could not find the sound in the dark.

Her feet came down the stone steps. One. Two. Three. Four. Five. Then a scrape. A small sharp sound. A smell of sulphur.

The match flared. Gran’s face came out of the dark — chin first, then her eyes, then the lines around her mouth. She held the match steady and looked at him.

“Still there,” she said.

She took the lamp from the shelf, lifted the glass, touched the match to the wick. The flame caught and grew. Yellow light filled the small room.

She held the lamp out to him. He took it. The glass was warm. The flame stood still inside.

He climbed the steps. The light went with him.
