The bakery was dark. The lane was dark. The whole village was dark.

Hana struck a match. The lamp in the window caught the flame and held it. Yellow light spread across the bench, the flour bin, the row of empty tins.

She rolled up her sleeves. The dough was cold from resting overnight. She pushed into it with the heels of her hands, folded it, pushed again. Her apron was white before she finished the first loaf.

The oven was warm. The heat came out in a wave when she opened the door. The first loaves went in. She worked the next batch while they baked.

When she pulled them out, the crust was brown and the smell filled the room. She set them on the bench to cool. The window was still dark outside.

She filled the window. Big loaves at the back. Small loaves at the front. A row of them, all the same colour, all warm.

She wiped the bench with a cloth. The flour was still on her apron.

Outside, the lane was no longer dark. A grey light was coming. No one had passed yet. But the window was ready.
