Mei lifted the lid of the wooden chest. Inside, on a bed of dark cloth, lay the tools.

She took out a brush. The bristles were stiff and short, worn at an angle. Mei ran her thumb across them.

Taro leaned in. "What is that for?"

Mei set the brush aside and picked up a chisel. The blade was thin, the edge sharp. The wooden handle was dark from years of gripping. "For the stones," she said.

She lifted a small hammer. The head was iron, the handle smooth. She held the two together — chisel and hammer — and Taro understood without being told.

Mei knelt beside the millstone. Its surface was scored with fine grooves, like a pattern of spokes. "When the grooves go flat," Mei said, "you dress the stone. You cut new lines with the chisel."

She did not strike the stone. She showed him how she would hold the chisel, how the hammer would tap, how the grooves would run in the right direction.

Gran stood in the doorway, watching. "The old miller left those," she said. "Each tool has its own shape. Its own wear."

Taro picked up the brush. The bristles were bent the way Mei's thumb had found them. He put it back in its place.
