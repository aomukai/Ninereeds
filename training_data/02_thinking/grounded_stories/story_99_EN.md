Emma heard it first—a small, quick sound from high up. She tugged Taro’s sleeve and pointed. The robin’s nest in the oak fork was different now. The eggs were gone.  

Taro scrambled onto the low branch, balancing on his knees. "There!" he whispered. A chick lifted its head, beak open wide. It made the sound again—thin, urgent.  

Gran came up behind them, her hands full of weeds. "Hatched last night," she said. "While we were sleeping."  

Emma watched the chick’s beak open and shut. It was so small. Yesterday there had been eggs, smooth and silent. Now this.  

"Where’s the mother?" Taro asked.  

"Gone for food," Gran said. "She’ll be back."  

The chick stretched its neck, wobbling. Emma held her breath. It was happening without them—the eggs had cracked, the nest had changed, the world had turned while no one was looking.  

Gran dropped the weeds into her basket. "Come away," she said. "Let them be."  

They stepped back. The sound followed them, sharp and new, all the way down the path.
