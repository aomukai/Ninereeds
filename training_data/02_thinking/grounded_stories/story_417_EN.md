Gran lifted the lamp. The flame moved behind the glass and the shadows jumped.

“Fetch me a potato,” she said. “From the sack at the bottom.”

Taro pulled the heavy door open and propped it with the iron bar. The stone steps were cold under his feet. The root cellar smelled of earth and apples and something darker underneath.

The hessian sack stood against the wall. He untied the string at the neck and reached inside.

His fingers touched a potato. Then another. Then one that gave way — soft, the skin loose and damp.

He pulled it out. The lamp light caught it. Dark brown at one end, wrinkled, with a patch of grey-green mould near the base.

He carried it up the steps.

Gran took it without saying anything. She turned it in her fingers, found the soft spot, cut it away with the knife. The rest of the potato was pale and firm.

“Still good,” she said. “But the soft one would have spread.”

She set the lamp on the table. Taro went back down and brought up the whole sack. They sat together on the kitchen floor, Gran with the knife, Taro with his hands. He sorted. She cut.

One potato was too far gone. Gran set it aside for the compost. The rest went back into the sack, each one checked, each one sound.
