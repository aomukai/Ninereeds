Gran lifted the sack of grain onto the low table by the hopper. The cloth was rough and the grain inside shifted when she set it down.

Mei stood on a wooden crate so she could see into the top of the millstones. The hopper was a wide wooden funnel, dark inside, with a narrow opening at the bottom.

Gran untied the sack and scooped the grain with a metal cup. She poured it into the hopper. The grain rattled as it fell.

Below, the millstones turned slowly against each other. The sound came up through the floor — a low, steady grind that Mei felt in her feet.

She stepped down and walked around to the chute where the flour came out. A fine white dust hung in the air. The cloth sack was half full already.

Mei touched the flour at the edge of the chute. It was warm.

Gran came down and put her hand into the sack. She let the flour run through her fingers. It was very fine — finer than sand, lighter than dust.

“From the same pile,” she said, nodding toward the grain sack. “But not the same thing.”

Mei put her own hand in. The flour was soft and warm and it clung to her skin. She watched it fall back into the sack, slow and white, and she did not say anything.
