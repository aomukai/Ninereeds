Taro grabbed the turnip leaves with both hands. He pulled.

The leaves tore off in his hands. He stumbled backward and sat down hard on the dirt.

The turnip stayed in the ground.

Emma knelt beside it. She pushed the broken stalks aside. The top of the turnip was as wide as her fist — pale white where the leaf had broken, pushing up through a crack in the soil.

"It's big," she said.

Taro got up and tried again. He wrapped his fingers around what was left of the stem and pulled. The turnip did not move.

Gran came out of the kitchen, wiping her hands on her apron. She looked at the broken leaves. She looked at the turnip top.

She went to the shed and came back with the garden fork.

"Stand back," she said.

She pushed the fork into the ground all around the turnip — once, twice, three times. She worked the handle back and forth. The soil loosened. Then she put the fork under the turnip and leaned.

The turnip came up. It was round and white and very wide — wider than Emma's whole hand.

Taro took it in both arms. It was heavy. He carried it to the kitchen, walking carefully, the white root pressed against his chest.

Gran filled a bowl with water. She set the turnip in and turned it around. The dirt washed away. The white showed clean.
