Gran sent them for bread. The basket hung on Taro's arm, light now, a loaf inside wrapped in cloth.

They came back along the side street. Vern's workshop door was open. Light spilled out onto the stone — yellow and warm against the grey winter afternoon.

They stopped. Just to look.

Vern was at the workbench. A stool stood upside down on the wood, one leg missing. He held a piece of fresh wood in his hand — pale, smooth at one end where the plane had been. He fitted it into the empty hole. It was too thick. He took it out, ran the plane along the side once, twice. Fine shavings curled and fell. He tried again. This time the leg went in square.

He did not look up. He turned the stool over and pressed down on it with both hands. Then he picked up a mallet.

Emma and Taro watched. The bread cooled in the basket. The shavings piled on the floor. The plane sat on the bench, its blade catching the light.

Vern tapped the leg home. He set the stool down on all four feet and rocked it gently. It did not rock.

Only then did he look up. He nodded once. They nodded back.

The errand took twice as long. Gran did not ask why.
