Gran gave Emma the list. “Eggs and a stamp.”

Emma folded it into her pocket. Taro was already at the gate. Biscuit was not coming — he was asleep in the sun by the kitchen door.

The lane was warm. The sun sat high and still. By the time they reached the village, the back of Emma’s neck was damp.

The general shop was cool inside. A bell rang when they opened the door. The eggs were in a box near the counter. Emma counted them into a paper bag. Taro bought the stamp at the till.

Outside, the street was bright again. A man was crouched beside a bicycle, turning a wheel with his fingers. Taro stopped to watch. Emma stopped too. The man did not look up. He turned the wheel again, then tapped a spoke with a small metal tool. Taro watched a long time.

Emma crossed to the green. The horse chestnut gave a wide circle of shade. She sat on the bench. The grass was short and dry. She found the clover without meaning to — her fingers touched it while she was looking at the man’s bicycle.

Four leaves.

She picked it. The stem was thin and pale green. She held it flat on her palm.

Taro came over. “He fixed it.”

“Good,” Emma said.

They walked home. The eggs were warm in the bag. Through the kitchen window, Gran’s arms were deep in dough. She did not look up. She was already kneading.
