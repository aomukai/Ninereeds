Gran lifted the lamp. The flame made the shadows jump.

“Onions,” she said. “For the stew. Taro, you are tall enough to reach.”

Taro stood under the rope. Onions hung from it in a long brown row. He took hold of the nearest one and pulled.

It did not come off.

He pulled harder. The onion stayed tight against the string.

“Twist it,” Mei said from the stairs.

Taro twisted. The string creaked. He pulled again — harder, quicker.

The string snapped.

Mei reached out and caught the first onion. Then the second. Then two more came down together. She caught them against her chest. One rolled off and stopped against her foot.

Gran held the lamp higher. The broken string hung in two pieces. Onions lay on the floor.

“Well,” Gran said. “That is one way to take them down.”

She picked up the broken rope and looked at the ends.

“I will rehang these tomorrow. For now, bring what you have. Three onions will do for the stew.”

Taro looked at the onions on the floor.

“Sorry,” he said.

Gran shook her head. “The string was old. You were pulling. That is all.”
