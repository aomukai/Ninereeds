Emma stood by the east wall, watching Gran’s hands move along the pear tree. The pears were small and hard, their stems clinging tight to the branches. Every morning, Gran pressed her thumb lightly against their skin, sniffed near the stem, then moved on.  

“Too soon,” she said.  

Three mornings passed. Then, as Emma reached up to touch one, Gran caught her wrist. “Wait.” She pressed her thumb—just once—and the flesh gave slightly. She bent close, inhaled. “Today.”  

They picked carefully, twisting each pear until the stem let go. The smell was sweet and green at once. Some were just out of reach; Gran shook her head. “Leave those.”  

Two days later, Emma found them in the grass beneath the wall—bruised, overripe, their skins split where they’d fallen. She gathered the least damaged into her skirt. Inside, Gran halved one with the kitchen knife. The flesh was pale, juice pooling on the board.  

“This was the right day,” Gran said. Not to Emma, not to the room—just to the pear in her hand.
