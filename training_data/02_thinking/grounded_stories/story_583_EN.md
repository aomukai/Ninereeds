The window was large. The glass was clean. Behind it, the loaves sat in a long, straight line.

Hana put the biggest loaves at the back. They were round and dark, with cuts across the top. Then the middle-sized ones. Then, right at the front, the smallest loaves — small and pale and shiny on top.

Emma pressed her nose close to the glass. “They look like stairs,” she said. “Big ones at the back. Little ones at the front.”

Mei stood beside her. She did not press her nose to the glass. She counted.

“One, two, three, four, five, six,” she said.

Emma counted too. Her finger touched the glass as she went. “One, two, three, four, five, six.”

They both got six.

Mei nodded. “Six is right.”

Inside, Hana turned from the window. Her apron was white. Flour dusted the front of it, and a smear of it crossed her cheek. She saw the two faces at the glass and smiled. She did not wave. She just smiled, and then she went back to the bench behind her.

The smell came through the glass — warm and brown and yeasty, the way bread smells when it has just come out of the oven.

Emma breathed it in. Mei breathed it in.

The loaves sat in their line, big at the back, small at the front, six of them, in order.
