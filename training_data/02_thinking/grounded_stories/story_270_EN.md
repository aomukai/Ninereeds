Vern’s workshop was warm. The stove ticked in the corner. Sawdust lay soft on the floor.

Emma held the wooden square. It was heavy in one hand, the two arms meeting at a perfect corner. She set it against the edge of the board she had cut.

There was a gap.

At the tip, a sliver of light showed between the square and the wood. Not much. But it was there.

Vern looked over without stopping what his hands were doing. “See it?”

“Yes,” Emma said.

“Then fix it.”

She carried the board to the bench. The vise held it still. She chose a thin chisel — smaller than her thumb — and shaved a whisper of wood from the corner. The shaving curled and fell.

She set the square again.

No gap.

The wood met the metal all the way along. The corner was a corner now.

Vern came over and put his finger on the join. He did not speak. He picked up a pencil and made a small mark on the board, near the edge. Then he went back to his work.

Emma left the square sitting on the bench, the two arms open, the corner perfect.
