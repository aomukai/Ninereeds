"Close your eyes," Vern said.

Mei closed them. The workshop sounds settled around her — the tick of cooling wood, the shuffle of shavings underfoot.

Something touched her nose. A piece of wood, held just there.

"Smell it."

She breathed in. Sharp. Sweet. Like the edge of a forest on a hot day.

"Pine," she said.

"Good."

The wood left. Another piece replaced it. She breathed again. This one was different — darker somehow, deeper. Dusty and old, like the floor of a place where nothing moved fast.

She hesitated.

"Oak," she said.

Vern did not say good this time. He put the oak in her hand. She turned it over. The grain was rough under her thumb. He put the pine in her other hand. The grain was straighter, the surface smoother.

"Most people only look," he said. "Your nose knows more than your eyes."

He picked up a third piece. She heard him lift it from the bench. Then the wood was at her nose again — almost no smell at all. Clean. Like air after rain.

She shook her head.

"Ash," Vern said. "Fresh-cut. It hides its smell until you work it."

He laid the three pieces side by side on the bench, grain up.

"Open your eyes."

She did. And saw what her nose had already told her.
