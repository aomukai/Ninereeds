Gran lifted the lamp from its hook by the kitchen door. She struck a match and lit the wick. The flame settled into a small, steady glow.

"Fetch the apples from the cellar," she said, handing the lamp to Emma. "The firm ones. I need six for a pie."

Emma took the lamp. The handle was warm from Gran's hand. She carried it across the kitchen, out the back door, and around to the wooden door set flat into the ground near the wall.

The cellar door was heavy. Emma propped it open with the iron bar, the way Gran had shown her. The stone steps were steep and worn in the middle. She went down carefully, holding the lamp high.

The smell of earth and stored things rose around her. Apples. Onions. Something sweet and close to turning.

The apple crates were along the wall. Emma set the lamp on a shelf and lifted the lid of the nearest one. Inside, the apples were packed in rows, each wrapped in newspaper. She lifted one out. It was round and hard in her hand. She turned it over. The skin was smooth. No soft spot. She checked the stem — it was still attached. She put it in the basket.

The next one had a dent near the bottom. She pressed it gently with her thumb. It gave a little. She set it on the shelf instead.

She chose six. Each one she checked — turning it, feeling the weight, looking for the bruise that would spoil the pie. When the basket was full, she picked up the lamp and climbed the steps back into the light.
