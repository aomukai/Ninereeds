Emma stood on tiptoe to push the letter into the red post box. The slot clicked shut.  

"It goes far away," Gran said, wiping flour from her hands. "Then the answer comes back the same way. Not tomorrow. Not the next day. Wait and see."  

Every morning after that, Emma checked the letterbox by Gran's gate. Sometimes there were bills. Sometimes nothing. The wood felt rough under her fingers.  

Weeks passed. The apple tree lost its blossoms. Then one morning, the envelope was different — crisp white, with a stamp from somewhere else. Emma ran inside, holding it by the corner.  

Gran slit it open with her thumb. The paper inside smelled faintly of ink and roads.  

"Same slot," Gran said, nodding toward the village. "Same post box. All that way and back."  

Emma touched the postmark. The journey was invisible, but the envelope was proof: it had happened.
