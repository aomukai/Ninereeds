Emma pressed her nose to the sitting-room window. The garden was sharp and bright—the apple tree’s branches, the hens pecking near the shed, even the veins on the cabbage leaves by the wall. The glass was cool against her skin.  

Gran reached past her and drew the lace curtain across. Suddenly the garden softened. The tree became a dark blur, the hens vanished, and only the faintest shapes moved behind the fabric. Light still came through—dappled, golden—but the picture was gone.  

Emma touched the curtain. The lace was stiff under her fingers. "I can’t see the tree properly now."  

Gran straightened the edge. "The light comes through," she said, "but the picture doesn’t."  

Outside, a breeze stirred the curtain. For a moment, the apple tree’s shadow swayed behind the lace—a smudge of branches, then nothing. Emma squinted. She could almost trace it, almost guess where the hens were scratching. Almost, but not quite.  

Gran moved away to the kitchen, leaving the curtain half-drawn. The room filled with watery light, bright but unclear, like sunlight through pond water.
