The jar sat on the kitchen windowsill, full of pond water and wriggling black commas. Emma pressed her nose to the glass. "They’re so small."  

"Wait," said Gran, kneading dough.  

Every morning they checked the pond. The tadpoles darted away from their shadows. "No legs yet," said Taro, disappointed.  

Weeks passed. The kitchen jar grew algae along its sides. One afternoon, Emma gasped. "Look!" Tiny nubs budded behind the gills.  

Gran peered over her shoulder. "Not frogs yet."  

The pond tadpoles changed slower. First one leg, then two, their tails shrinking as their bodies rounded. Taro poked the water with a stick. "Why won’t they hurry up?"  

"Takes as long as it takes," said Gran, watching a dragonfly skim the surface.  

By midsummer, the jar held miniature frogs clinging to the glass. At the pond, full-grown frogs plopped into the water at their approach. Emma crouched by the flat stone. One sat very still, throat pulsing.  

Gran wiped floury hands on her apron. "Every year," she said, "it feels like a long time."
