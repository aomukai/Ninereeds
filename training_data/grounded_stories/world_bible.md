# Ninereeds Story World — Generation Guide

This document is the canonical reference for all grounded story generation.
Every story DeepSeek writes for this corpus must be consistent with the cast,
locations, and writing rules below.

---

## The Cast

### Emma
A girl, around eight years old. Curious and careful — she notices small things
(a crack in the bark, a trail of ants, the way dew sits on a leaf). She likes
picking things up and turning them over in her hands. She asks questions when
something puzzles her.

### Taro
A boy, same age as Emma. More physical and impulsive — he climbs before he
thinks, jumps into puddles without checking how deep, runs ahead on the path.
But he listens when Gran explains something. His full name in Japanese is 太郎;
in English and German stories he is Taro.

### Gran
Emma and Taro's grandmother. She lives in the stone cottage. She is the one
who knows what things are and what they become — she tells them that the acorn
grows into an oak, that the cocoon will open, that the clouds coming from the
west mean rain by afternoon. She does not lecture. She answers questions while
doing something else: kneading dough, pulling weeds, hanging laundry.

### Biscuit
A medium-sized brown dog. He belongs to the cottage and follows whoever is
going somewhere interesting. He sniffs everything, barks at squirrels, splashes
through puddles without hesitation, and carries sticks back that are too big
for him. He cannot speak but his actions often move the story forward — he
finds the squirrel hole, he pulls toward the pond, he comes back soaked and
shakes water everywhere.

## Anchor Locations

All stories are set in or near these five places. They are always described
consistently — same features, same physical details, same sensory qualities.

### 1. The Oak
A very old oak tree at the edge of Gran's field, where the grass meets the
trees. It has one low branch thick enough to sit on, about shoulder height on
a child. The roots are large and raised; a squirrel has a hole between two of
them at the base. The bark is rough and deeply furrowed. In autumn the ground
around it is covered in acorns and dead leaves. In winter it is bare. In spring
it has small bright-green leaves. A robin sometimes nests in the fork higher up.

**Appears in:** oak tree, rainy day (shelter under it), bird's nest (the upper
fork), autumn leaves, oak in snow.

### 2. Gran's House
A low stone cottage at the end of a lane. The kitchen has a wooden table, a
heavy iron pot that hangs over the fire, a window over the sink looking out
onto the garden, and a smell of bread or soup almost always. The garden behind
the cottage has a vegetable patch, an apple tree, a stone bench, and a low
wooden gate. The garden shed leans against the back wall and is full of tools.

**Appears in:** kitchen cooking, baking, morning routine, getting dressed,
bedtime, rainy window, winter evening, garden, apple orchard, birthday,
building/making (shed), morning garden.

### 3. The Pond
A shallow pond at the bottom of the field, a short walk from the oak. It has
reeds along one edge, a flat stone good for sitting on, and a willow that
dips into the water on the far side. Frogs sit on the lily pads in summer.
A pair of ducks nests in the reeds in spring. The water is still enough to
reflect the sky when there is no wind.

**Appears in:** pond life, fishing, picnic (blanket on the grass beside it).

### 4. The Meadow Path
A narrow path through long grass that connects Gran's garden gate to the pond
and, further along, to the school. In summer the grass on either side is tall
enough to hide Biscuit. Wildflowers grow in the verges — dandelions, daisies,
clover. The path is muddy after rain. Butterflies cross it in warm weather.

**Appears in:** rainy day (the path floods and turns to mud), meadow/butterfly,
dog walk, body and touch (children run and fall here), farm animals (the field
beside it has sheep).

### 5. The Schoolyard
A walled yard at the far end of the meadow path. A green wooden gate, always
slightly stiff. A climbing frame in one corner, a bench along the wall, a big
ash tree that gives shade in summer. The school bell can be heard from the
meadow path on still mornings.

**Appears in:** schoolyard, playground.

## Seasonal Continuity

The oak, the pond, the meadow path, and Gran's garden change with the seasons.
Stories reference the same physical features across seasons to build a
persistent world. Examples:

- The squirrel's hole at the oak base appears in the autumn acorn story, is
  buried under snow in the winter story, and Biscuit sniffs at it in spring.
- The robin's nest in the oak fork is empty in autumn, built in spring, holds
  eggs in early summer.
- The pond is clear and full of frogs in summer; iced at the edges in winter.
- Gran's apple tree is in blossom in spring, heavy with fruit in autumn.

## Writing Rules

### Reading level
Grade 1–2. Short sentences. Concrete actions. A child of seven should be
able to follow every sentence without explanation.

### Prose style: Beatrix Potter, not textbook
The world teaches itself through what happens, not through definitions.
Characters act and notice things. Objects appear through touch, smell, sound,
and weight — not through labels.

RIGHT:
  She turned it in her fingers. The cap was rough. The bottom was smooth.
  Biscuit pressed his nose into the hole and sneezed.
  The soup smelled of onion and something warm she couldn't name.

WRONG:
  "This is an acorn," said Gran. "It is small and round."
  Emma said, "I am holding an acorn."
  Taro learned that acorns come from oak trees.

### Pronouns
Use pronouns freely when the referent is clear and local. "She turned it in
her fingers" is correct because the reader knows who she is and what it is.
Do not use pronouns when the referent has not been introduced in the last
two sentences.

### Dialogue
Natural, brief, purposeful. People speak the way they actually speak to
children — not explaining, but reacting, pointing, asking. Gran does not
deliver information; she answers a question while doing something else.

RIGHT:  "Bury it," Gran said, without looking up from the weeds.
WRONG:  "That is an acorn," Gran explained. "If you plant it, it will grow
         into an oak tree just like this one."

### Sentence variety
Mix very short sentences with longer ones. Short sentences land facts and
actions. Longer sentences carry motion or atmosphere.

  The acorn was small. It fit inside Emma's fist.
  Biscuit ran ahead and came back and ran ahead again, nose low to the ground.

### Length
150–220 words per story. This is not a word count target — write the scene
fully and stop when it is done. Do not pad. Do not summarise at the end.

### What not to do
- No moral at the end.
- No "and they all went home happy."
- No character announcing what they have learned.
- No textbook constructions in any language (私はビルです / I am a boy / Das ist ein Apfel).
- No exposition about the world that a child in the scene would already know.
