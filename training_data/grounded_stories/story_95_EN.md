Emma crouched by the vegetable patch. The leaves brushed her knees—broad, green, taller than her hand. She touched one. It felt warm from the sun, the veins ridges under her fingers.  

Gran knelt beside her, tugging a weed from between the rows. "Remember?" she said.  

Emma did. The soil had been bare then, dark and crumbly. She'd pressed the seeds in one by one, counting under her breath. Now the plants stood shoulder to shoulder, leaves overlapping, stems thick as pencils.  

"It's the same row," Emma said.  

Gran nodded. She snapped a pea pod from its stem and split it open. The peas inside were small, bright green, lined up tight.  

Emma took one. It tasted like the smell of rain.  

Biscuit pushed past them, sniffing at the base of the plants. His tail wagged against Emma's leg.  

The row was full now. The seeds had gone. The leaves rustled when the wind came.
