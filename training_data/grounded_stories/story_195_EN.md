Gran’s hands pushed the dough away, then folded it back. The heel of her palm pressed down, turned, pressed again. The dough made a soft, sticky sound.  

Emma stood on her tiptoes to see better. She tried to copy—push, fold, press—but the dough stuck to her fingers. Gran’s hands moved quick and certain. Emma’s moved slow, uncertain.  

Gran didn’t look up. Her hands knew what to do. The lines on her knuckles deepened as she worked.  

“How do you know?” Emma asked.  

Gran kept kneading. “Practice,” she said.  

Emma watched. The dough was smooth under Gran’s hands, rough under hers. She tried again—push, fold, press—and this time the dough moved a little better.  

Gran’s hands never stopped. They had done this before. They knew.  

Emma studied her own fingers, then Gran’s. One day, she thought. One day.  

The dough rose in the bowl between them. The kitchen smelled of flour and warmth. Gran’s hands kept working. Emma’s tried to follow.
