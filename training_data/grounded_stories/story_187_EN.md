The lane was already dark when they turned the corner. Emma could see Gran’s lamp in the kitchen window, a yellow square against the grey stone.  

"It was light when we left," Taro said, kicking a pebble. It skittered ahead, lost in the shadows before it stopped.  

Gran was waiting at the gate, her sleeves rolled to the elbow. "Shortest day," she said, wiping flour from her hands. "Four o’clock and the sun’s given up."  

Inside, the kitchen smelled of bread and warmth. Emma hung her coat on the peg. The walk had taken no longer than usual—past the baker’s, up the hill, past the dip where the puddle was frozen at the edges—but outside, the world had folded into dusk halfway home.  

Taro pressed his nose to the window. "Same walk," he said.  

"Different light," Gran answered, lifting the loaf from the oven. The crust crackled.  

Emma touched the glass. Cold seeped through her fingertips. Beyond it, the lane was a dark seam between the hedges, the same and not the same.
