The garden gate was old, its wood grey and slightly warped. Biscuit trotted up, sniffed at the latch, then turned and slipped sideways through the narrow gap beside the left post. His ribs brushed the wood as he wriggled through, tail vanishing into the garden.  

Taro eyed the gap. It was barely wider than his shoulders. He turned sideways like Biscuit, edged one foot through, then his hip. The wood pressed against his chest. He sucked in his stomach and pushed—  

And stuck.  

His shoulders jammed between the post and the gate. He couldn’t go forward. He tried to twist back, but the wood scraped his elbows. Biscuit sat on the other side, ears cocked, watching.  

"Gran!" Taro called, but she was inside. He wriggled backward, scraping his shirt, until he popped free. The gate creaked as he unlatched it properly and stepped through. Biscuit gave him a look, then trotted off toward the apple tree, leaving the gap empty behind him.  

Taro rubbed his elbow. The gap stayed just wide enough for a dog.
