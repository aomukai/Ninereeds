The gate creaked as Gran pushed it open. Beyond the meadow path, the field sloped down to the neighbor’s barn. A low pen stood near the fence, and inside, woolly shapes moved—small ones, unsteady on their legs.  

Emma crouched. "They’re like Biscuit," she whispered. "But softer."  

Taro reached through the fence. A lamb wobbled closer, its nose damp, breath warm against his fingers. It bleated—a tiny, wavering sound.  

Gran lifted the bucket. "Hay and feed," she said. The sheep shuffled forward, their thick wool brushing the wooden rails. The air smelled of straw and milk.  

One lamb nosed Emma’s sleeve. Its ears twitched. She laughed.  

"Careful," Gran said. "They’re still learning their feet."  

Taro scooped a handful of hay. The lamb nibbled, then sneezed. Biscuit whined by the gate, tail thumping the dirt.  

The farmer waved from the barn. Gran nodded. "Time to let them rest," she said.  

The gate creaked shut behind them. The lambs huddled close to their mothers, their small backs rising and falling in the sun.
