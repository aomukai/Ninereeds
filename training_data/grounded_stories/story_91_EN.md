Emma and Taro ran down the meadow path that morning. The earth was dry and hard underfoot, the grass crisp at the edges. Biscuit’s paws left faint dents in the dust.  

"Remember how it looks now," Gran said, pinching rosemary by the garden gate.  

After lunch, the sky had emptied itself. They stepped out again. The path was the same path—but the dip where the stones sank held a long, shining puddle. Taro jumped in. Mud splattered his ankles, dark and sudden against his socks.  

Emma crouched by the verge. The wildflowers bent low, their stems slick. Where dry clover had crunched under fingers earlier, now wet leaves left cool streaks on her palm.  

Gran waited by the gate, her sleeves rolled to the elbow. "Still the meadow path," she said.  

Taro scuffed his toe through the mud, carving a line that filled at once with water. Biscuit sniffed at the new marks, then shook his paws one by one.  

The puddle reflected the oak’s bare branches now, where before it had only been dust and the memory of rain.
