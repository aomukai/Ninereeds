Biscuit stretched out on the rug by the fire, his paws twitching in sleep. The rug was his—worn smooth in the middle, warm from the flames. He had claimed it the first night, curling into the hollow his own weight had made over years.  

Bello sniffed along the kitchen wall, nose to the floorboards. The draft from under the door was slight, but the stones held the day’s heat longer than they should. He circled once, twice, then folded himself into the corner by the hinge.  

Gran kneaded dough at the table. She glanced at Biscuit, then at Bello, and said nothing.  

By the third evening, Biscuit went straight to the rug without looking. Bello waited until the fire had burned low, then padded to his spot. The kitchen door creaked when the wind blew, but the corner stayed warm.  

On the seventh night, Gran banked the coals and blew out the lamp. Two shapes in the dark: one by the fire, one by the door. Neither stirred when she stepped over them.
