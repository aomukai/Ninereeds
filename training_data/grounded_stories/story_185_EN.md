Emma reached up. The apple came away with a soft snap.  

"It was a flower," she said, turning it in her hands. The skin was warm from the sun.  

Gran stood on the ladder, her basket half full. "Same tree," she said. "All year."  

Emma remembered spring. The branches had been white with blossom, buzzing with bees. She had stood underneath and let the petals fall on her hair.  

In summer, the apples were small and hard. She had pressed her thumb against one, testing.  

Now the branches bent low. The fruit was heavy, red-gold where the sun touched it.  

She put the apple in the basket with the others. The smell rose up—sweet, a little sharp.  

Winter came to her suddenly: the tree black and bare against the sky, the ground frost-hard.  

Gran stepped down, brushing a leaf from her sleeve. "Next year," she said, "it'll do it all again."  

Emma touched the rough bark. The tree stood quiet, holding its seasons inside.
