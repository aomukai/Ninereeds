She put the apples in a row on a shelf. "Why do you save them?" the girl asked. The grandmother pointed to the pantry door. "Because soon it will be cold. Then no apples will grow. So I keep them now to eat when the snow comes." The girl touched an apple. It was smooth and round. She understood: conserving means saving something now so you can use it later. The grandmother smiled. Then she took a mitten from her pocket. "I am conserving this too," she said. "The old mitten has a hole. But I can fix it, not throw it away. That is also conserving." The girl nodded. She saw a cracked bowl on the shelf. "What about that bowl?" she asked. "I am still thinking," the grandmother said. "I must not just see it as broken. Deriving a new use from it would be best. If I just throw it away, I am distorting what I have. Instead I will categorize it with the other things to fix." The girl smiled. She could see that conserving was not only about apples. She looked at the rooster. It was fracturing a dry crust on the ground. Even the rooster was conserving.
