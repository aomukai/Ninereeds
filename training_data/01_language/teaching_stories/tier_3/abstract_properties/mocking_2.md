Why does mocking matter?

A boy walked to the well with a wavy bucket. He saw a girl by the hedge, and she pointed at his bucket. “Clank, clank,” she said in a sneaky voice, imitating the sound. Then she copied his walk—her steps were long and wobbly. The boy stopped. So she stopped. He picked up his bucket again. The girl laughed, loud like a goat. That was mocking: making a face or a voice to show you are not sery about someone else.

At the market, a man dropped a jar of salsa. Red sauce spread on the ground. Two children saw it. One child widened her eyes and made her mouth round—exaggerated. The other child bent his hand to copy the man’s grab for the jar. Then both children laughed. The man’s face went still. He did not invent a funny story. He just cleaned the mud. The children were mocking him, because a stumble is not a joke for the one who falls.

An old woman sat on her doorstep. She saw the children from the field. “Come here,” she said. The children came. “You made face and hands for the man,” she said. “That is normalizing to you, but not to him. Mocking tells a story: ‘I am better than you.’ But it is a sneaky lie. Look at the sky—a cloud can change shape, but it is still a cloud. A person stumbles. Then he gets up. That is all.” The children looked at their feet. They did not laugh again.

Why does mocking matter? Because laughter after a stumble can build a wall between two people. Or you can choose not to imitate the voice. Then the wall stays small, and you can help the one who fell.
