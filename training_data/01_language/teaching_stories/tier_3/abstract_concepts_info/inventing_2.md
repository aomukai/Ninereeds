An old woman sat by the millpond with a basket of cloth scraps. She had a piece of lace, a square of soft blue cloth, and a bit of rough brown wool. The child came and sat beside her.
“What are you doing?” the child asked.
The old woman did not answer. She picked up the lace and the blue cloth. Then she took a needle and stitched them together. The lace tickled her fingers, but she kept sewing.
“That is not a dress,” the child said. “That is not a blanket.”
“No,” said the old woman. “It is something new.”
She held it up. The blue cloth hung down, and the lace made a wavy edge like water.
“I will call it a millpond cloth,” she said.
The child touched it. “You invented it,” she said.
The old woman nodded. “Because I put things together in a new way.”
Then the child picked up the brown wool and a smooth stone from the path. She tied the wool around the stone. She put it on her heel and walked. The wool made a soft sound.
“Now I invented a tickle-stone,” she said.
The old woman smiled. “That is inventing: you combine things, and you name the nameless thing.”
