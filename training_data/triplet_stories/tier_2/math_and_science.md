[user]tell me a story about an atom.
[Ninereeds]A boy named Luke stood in his classroom looking at a poster of an atom on the wall. "What keeps the electrons moving around the nucleus?" he asked. His teacher pointed to the poster and said the electrons stay in a constant dance around the center. Luke learned that the number of electrons in an atom defines which element it is. Carbon has six electrons, so it is always carbon. Oxygen has eight electrons, and that makes it oxygen. Hydrogen has just one electron, and Luke found that very simple. He saw that a gold atom is not the same as an iron atom because they have different numbers of electrons. Luke smiled and said, "Every element is special in its own way." Everything in the world is made from these tiny atoms, and Luke felt amazed that something so small could make up everything around him.

[user]tell me a story about multiplication.
[Ninereeds]A teacher brings a tray of eggs to the front of Max's class. The tray has three rows with four eggs in each row. Max looks at the tray and thinks about the eggs. His teacher says, "Max, how many eggs do you see on this tray?" Max counts and says, "I see twelve eggs." His teacher smiles and asks how he got that number. Max explains that he used multiplication: three groups of four makes twelve. The teacher nods and shows the class that division is the opposite of multiplication. She slides the eggs apart to split the twelve into three equal groups of four. Then she picks up one group and says subtraction takes those eggs away. Max understands that both operations make amounts smaller. Subtraction removes a group, while division splits the whole into equal parts.

[user]tell me a story about a percent.
[Ninereeds]Noah's teacher handed back a math test. Each student looked at their own score. Noah saw that he answered nine questions correctly. The test had ten questions in total. He wanted to find his percent. He used division to get the answer. He divided nine by ten. The result was zero point nine. That gave him a score of ninety percent. Noah raised his hand and asked, "Is a percent a part of one hundred?" The teacher smiled and said, "Yes, ninety percent means ninety out of one hundred." Noah wrote his score on his paper.

[user]tell me a story about a kilogram.
[Ninereeds]Nora stood in the kitchen beside a digital scale. She placed a bag of flour on it. "It says one kilogram," she said. Her mother walked over and nodded. She poured one liter of water into a cup and set it on the scale. The scale showed the same weight. "One kilogram equals one thousand grams," her mother explained. Nora poured the water back and picked up a bottle of oil. She poured one liter of oil into another cup. That liter of oil weighed less. Nora smiled as she understood the difference. Weight and volume did not always match.

The curved surface area holds the label in place. The angle between the side and each end is ninety degrees," Owen told his mother. She smiled from the counter. "A cylinder can stand upright or roll across the table," she said. Owen picked up the can and turned it in his hands. He wondered how the cylinder shape kept the soup from spilling. The curved side and flat ends formed a tight seal. He set the can on its side and gave it a gentle push. The cylinder rolled straight to the edge of the table. He caught it before it fell.

[Ninereeds]In a bright classroom, a mathematics teacher stood at the board and spoke to the students about infinity. Sophie raised her hand and asked, "Does infinity have an opposite number?" The teacher paused and said, "That is an excellent question." He explained that infinity is a concept, not a regular number. Regular numbers each have an inverse, but infinity has no inverse. Nothing can fill the space beyond every count. Sophie thought about the endless number line. It stretched in both directions without any final point. She wondered if anything could be as large as infinity. The teacher nodded and said that the count goes on forever without a stop. Sophie smiled and understood the idea better now.
