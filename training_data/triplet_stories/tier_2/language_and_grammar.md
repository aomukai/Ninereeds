[user]tell me a story about a conjunction.
[Ninereeds]A teacher stands at the front of a classroom with a chalkboard. A boy named Will sits at his desk and watches the teacher carefully. The teacher picks up a piece of chalk and writes a sentence on the board. She turns to the class and explains that a conjunction joins two parts of a sentence. "Can someone tell me what a conjunction does?" she asks. Will raises his hand and says, "A conjunction connects words or groups of words together." The teacher smiles and nods at Will. She writes a contraction on the board next to the first sentence. She explains that a contraction turns two words into a shorter form. Will writes both examples in his notebook. He practices writing sentences with conjunctions and contractions. The lesson helps him understand how to make his writing flow better.

[Ninereeds]A teacher writes a preposition on the board. Cody raises his hand and asks a question about the word. "How does a preposition show the location of a thing?" he asks. The teacher picks up a small rubber ball from the desk. The ball rests under a chair across the room. Cody watches the ball in its place. [user]tell me a story about suffix.
[Ninereeds]Ella sits in the front row of the classroom. The teacher writes a suffix on the chalkboard. Ella watches the chalk make white marks on the dark board. "Today we will learn about the suffix -ful," the teacher says. Ella raises her hand and asks, "Does -ful change a word into something full of that thing?" The teacher nods and says, "That is correct, Ella." She adds the suffix to the word "joy" and the new word becomes "joyful." Ella writes the word in her notebook. She likes how one small suffix can make a whole new word. The vocabulary of the classroom grows with each example. The students practice making many new words at their desks. Ella understands that learning grammar helps her build better sentences. She smiles and closes her notebook at the end of the lesson.

[user]tell me a story about a stanza.
[Ninereeds]Emma sits at her desk in class and opens her notebook. The teacher shows a poem on the board. Emma sees that the poem is split into small groups of lines. "Those groups are called stanzas," her teacher says. Emma raises her hand and asks how stanzas are different from paragraphs. "Stanzas are for poetry, and paragraphs are for prose," the teacher explains. Emma understands that prose tells a narrative in long flowing sentences. A narrative grows across many paragraphs from start to finish. Each stanza holds just a few lines that fit together as one strong part. The poet writes each stanza to build the poem like a picture made of words. Emma reads the poem again and feels the rhythm of each group. She writes her own stanza in her notebook. The words fit together on the page.
