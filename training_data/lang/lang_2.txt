# NOUN RULES

Nouns split into:

- concrete noun
- abstract noun
- event noun

# CONCRETE NOUN TEMPLATES

## N1 — subject + verb
Use when noun can act physically.

Examples:
The dog runs.
The bird flies.
The child laughs.

Rules:

- subject must plausibly perform the action
- prefer biologically or physically natural actions
- avoid overly specific activities

Allowed for:
animals, humans, vehicles, natural objects with motion

Forbidden:
“The stone thinks.”
“The grass explains.”

## N2 — noun + property

Use when noun has stable observable property.

Examples:
The stone is heavy.
The water is cold.
The road is long.

Rules:

- adjective must describe inherent or stable property
- prefer sensory properties

Allowed:
size, temperature, weight, color, shape

Avoid:
“The chair is philosophical.”

## N3 — noun + environmental action

Examples:
The fire burns.
The rain falls.
The wind blows.

Rules:

- use natural processes
- no intentional agency required

# ABSTRACT NOUN TEMPLATES

Abstract nouns need different handling.

“Ability”, “abstraction”, “absence”, “acceptance” are not physical actors.

Do NOT force them into concrete-agent templates.

## A1 — abstract noun + copula + adjective

Examples:
Acceptance is important.
Accuracy is valuable.
Abstraction is difficult.

Rules:

- adjective must evaluate or characterize concept
- prefer cognitive/social usefulness

## A2 — abstract noun + affects + noun

Examples:
Absence causes sadness.
Accuracy improves results.
Acceptance builds trust.

Rules:

- causal relation must be intuitive
- object should be human-scale and understandable

This is extremely valuable for reasoning.

## A3 — human + has + abstract noun

Examples:
The student has ability.
The leader has accountability.
The scientist has accuracy.

Rules:

- abstract noun behaves as possessed quality
- use socially plausible pairings

Very important for grounding abstractions into agents.

# VERB RULES

Verbs split into:

- physical action
- mental action
- state/process verb

Use dictionary/base meaning only.

## V1 — subject + verb

Examples:
The child runs.
The woman writes.
The bird moves.

Rules:

- keep objectless if action is naturally complete
- prefer high-frequency physical actions

## V2 — subject + verb + adverb

Examples:
The boy writes carefully.
The girl speaks softly.
The dog moves slowly.

Rules:

- adverb must modify manner
- avoid abstract adverbs initially

Excellent for grounding action quality.

## V3 — gerund + copula + adjective

Examples:
Running is tiring.
Reading is useful.
Swimming is difficult.

Rules:

- adjective must describe experience or utility
- avoid metaphorical evaluations

This is VERY important structurally.

## V4 — subject + verb + purpose

Examples:
People run to exercise.
Birds fly to travel.
We eat to live.

Rules:

- purpose must be simple and direct
- infinitive purpose only

Excellent for causal cognition.

# ADJECTIVE RULES

Adjectives split into:

- physical property
- emotional/cognitive state
- evaluative adjective

## ADJ1 — adjective + noun + action

Examples:
The careful worker checks the machine.
The heavy stone falls quickly.
The sharp knife cuts paper.

Rules:

- adjective should influence action naturally
- property should matter causally

This is one of the strongest templates.

## ADJ2 — adjective noun + copula

Examples:
Careful consideration is important.
Accurate information is useful.
Abnormal behavior is dangerous.

Rules:

- noun should match adjective semantically
- avoid philosophical abstraction early

## ADJ3 — comparative structure

Examples:
The river is wider than the road.
The elephant is larger than the dog.

Rules:

- use physically comparable entities
- avoid abstract comparison initially

Important for relational reasoning.

# SPECIAL RULES

- only use words from allowlist.txt

# GERUND / PARTICIPLE WORDS

Words like:
abbreviating
absorbing
accepting
accumulating

can behave as:

verbs
adjectives

Allowed templates:

participle adjective

The absorbing book is popular.
The accepting teacher helps students.

gerund abstraction

Accumulating takes time.
Accepting is difficult.

Avoid forcing unclear interpretations.

# MULTIWORD EXPRESSIONS

Example:
blade of grass
block of wood

Treat as atomic nouns.

Allowed:
The blade of grass moves.
The block of wood is heavy.

Forbidden:
splitting internal structure during generation.

# FORBIDDEN OUTPUTS

Never generate:

- category errors
- metaphor
- symbolic/figurative language
- emotionally manipulative content
- nonsensical agency
- ontology recursion

Examples to forbid:
“Abstraction eats food.”
“The absence runs.”
“The grass understands.”
“Accuracy is green.”
